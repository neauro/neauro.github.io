Monterey

Aug 29th, 2011

This past weekend my boyfriend came down to visit. After much ado, we managed to obtain a rental car. Who knew it was so difficult to rent cars when you’re younger than 25 and don’t have a credit card? Age and the ability to spend money you don’t have presupposes responsibility and maturity, I guess.

Though, I’m fine paying an extra $25 for the privilege of being young and having a relatively smaller amount of responsibilities. :D

derpaka & shibaru on the dashboard

The day alternated between fog and sunshine.

starting out

aaaand some 20 miles later

What’s visible from the highways of California always impresses me on a level that I can’t quite explain. I love how the environment seems to change from mile to mile, how one state can be all at once chilly and arid, all farms and then all dunes, here grass and there ice plants as far as the eye can see.

in retrospect, why didn’t we stop here?!

Eventually we arrived at destination point #1, my childhood home. Here is where I had my first cat and my first birds, where I rejected my first Barbie doll and watched things like KaBlam! and Sonic the Hedgehog until unreasonable hours of the morning.

one of the cars outside this place actually had WA plates

Then we moved on to our true destination, the Monterey Bay Aquarium. I wish that I had remembered more of it, or more of the area in general; unfortunately a lot has been lost in time and memory. The one area that I for sure remembered, the exhibit called Beautiful But Deadly at the top of one of the MBA staircases, was now something else like Enchanted Kelp Forest.

spotted jellyfish

Because a lot of the exhibits were dark, I had a lot of trouble taking pictures of most things, and though I took my telephoto lens I ended up using my 35mm f/1.8 for the majority of the pictures, just so I could grab the light. In general I had a good time, though the place was swarming with children and parents taking pictures of things, sometimes with a desperateness that was mind-boggling. Do you really need a photo of your kids at every exhibit? And sometimes the fervor with which people took photos of exhibits before even taking time to understand or really look at what was going on in them was frustrating.

Anyway, this is coming from a person who really only enjoyed the aquarium for all the good photo opportunities, so.

Wading Through the Amazon Careers Website

Aug 23rd, 2011

The other day I tried applying to a job through the Amazon Careers website, and the experience was pretty interesting.

The normal Amazon Careers website has a frame to the right which is the container for search results and job details.

aww yeah.  so much opportunity for employment


the first listing

Unfortunately for me at the time, I was given a direct link to an internship which filled up the entire page.

does the font need to be that small?

I think the link itself is deprecated because I couldn’t find the job by searching through Amazon Careers, but I didn’t know this at the time. And the last time I tried to apply for a job to Amazon before this was a couple months ago, so I already had a profile, albeit completely unrelated to the position that I was applying for now. Still, I pressed “Apply Now,” thinking that I could change that information before submitting.

But the next page that showed up said, merely,

wait…what about the first three steps?!

“Step 4 of 4” could only mean “This is the last step before submitting your application,” so I applaud the transparency of it.  Still, for some reason I went and clicked through anyway, thinking that I’d still be able to change what irrelevant or old information that I had on file. I didn’t. Ack!

So I searched in haste for “My Account” to put in my application information. When I eventually found it It looks like this:

unexpectedly sparse

The text is, to me, a little small for a page that has nothing else on it. I guess it’s easily remedied by just increasing the font size.  (Also, is that a dangling list element?)

The “Your Profile” page is similar. This is where you fill in all the information that will be used to apply to whatever job at Amazon that you so desire.

this is everything Amazon needs to know about you.
name, address, and the last three degrees you earned (not pictured)

So then I uploaded my profile, and…what?  I’m still not quite sure whether the updated information is being used for the application that I had already submitted. I imagine that it is, but even so, a confirmation would be nice.

These are the things that I wish had been different about this process in general:
  • I want to see what information you’re submitting to a job. Or, at least, a recap of what steps 1, 2, and 3 had been before I went through step 4. Even now, months after first creating my account, I can’t reconstruct what the previous steps would have been. “Sign up, Fill in Information, Find Job and Apply?”
  • I want the ability to apply to jobs that are completely different in nature. In particular, I wish that I had been able to submit different resumes to different jobs, or that I could provide a cover letter for each one. (I normally hate writing cover letters, but this position in particular was one that I enjoyed writing a cover letter for. Ah, wasted effort!
  • I want less fields about what school you went to on your Profile page. How many applicants does Amazon have every day that have been to three post-high school schools? Are there enough to justify making everyone go through filling this spaces in?
  • I want a notification about selection or non-selection of a position.  The whole process is pretty automatic-feeling anyway, so having a confirmation e-mail that an application was selected, or an e-mail gently letting down your hopes would be nice, haha.

As a note, in order to get the “Step 4 of 4” screenshot I pretended I was applying to another arbitrary job, and the application still went through though I pressed “back” rather than the final “Submit.” Out of curiosity, I just checked its status on the “Your Account” page.

it reads, Reviewed, Not Selected.
oh well, i guess


Which makes me think:
  • Wow, someone reviewed my application fast!
  • Or else the computer just read through my resume quickly and tossed it out?
  • Wait, why are the other positions I applied to not showing up?  Did my real application not actually go through?  Or does this status thing just expire after a week or so?
  • Also, why does my application for the Design Intern position still say that my application is already on file, when this page obviously doesn’t have it listed?

In conclusion: I have a weird feeling that people don’t really get jobs through career websites, but through career fairs and internal connections, so I suspect the entire site is just a front anyway, haha.

Edit: Oh!  I did receive an automated “We received your application!” e-mail for the job I arbitrarily applied to just to obtain a screenshot.  But it still reads “Reviewed, Not Selected” on the status page.

Zoos and Animartyrs

Aug 21st, 2011

Chris came to visit! And we visited San Francisco Zoo because I have wanted to and have not had buddies nearby that enjoy the act of going to the zoo enough to just go and bear with my taking pictures of everything.

(Full set of zoo pictures is here.) This time, though, I didn’t take hundreds of pictures like I normally do, probably because I’ve been to Woodland Park Zoo in the past couple months and because I’m restricting myself to RAW now, which means less space for pictures. If I couldn’t see anything but an animal’s back then I usually just passed. Being so conservative probably wasn’t complete necessary since I had about 300/500 shots left at the end of the day, but, so it goes.

cool story bro

Zoos are always kind of tough because on one hand, animals are great! And on the other hand, these animals are trapped here in small exhibits, pacing and staring out the wires and sifting the mud over and over again for…what?

something.  anything

The primates in particular always depress me, probably because they look so human. And the flamingos by one of the dining areas would constantly spread their wings and then flap them all together, as if trying to rise up as one.

oh….right

The zoo was, as usual, filled with children, trolling seagulls, nervously approaching goats at the petting zoo, listening vaguely as parents sometimes asked them questions about the world around them and taught them things about the animals that they read on the supplied placards by each exhibit. Information everywhere; not like the internet, but for easy perusal relevant to the current circumstances of one’s physical presence. Eating lunch with Chris beside the flamingo pool, I looked down and saw that my burger was in a box lined with yet more information. An explanation of why three of the nine subspecies of tigers are extinct. Encouragements to eat organic and local.

ha!  i see what you did there!
(source)


I can’t even think of any other places where people will happily go to be informed about something that is not really relevant to their everyday life, much less have a good chance of taking that information, finding it reasonable and un-offensive, and considering it, and acting upon it.  Science museums are different, because everyone agrees science is good; at the very least, someone would more easily agree to the idea that science is good, than to agreeing that “eating less meat is good,” or “ensuring impoverished people are not exploited for our benefit is good.”

In any case, the quote in the Children’s Zoo seemed pretty legit – I don’t remember it exactly, but in essence it went, ”Teach children how to love something and they will work to protect it.” The gorillas and the flamingos are martyrs. The kid screaming at his half-finished Cheezit and bending down to pick it up and replace it in his mouth is a potential future warrior of the earth.

GamesJS Kick-Off Meeting

Aug 19th, 2011

A couple nights ago I attended the GamesJS Kickoff Meeting, at which were speakers discussing the advantages of game development in HTML5 and JavaScript. It was held at IGN. The place turned out to be filled with a lot of indie game devs, and a lot of the advantages toted were that of the cross-platformability of games built using HTML5 and JavaScript, which can go on phones and computers without any worries of Flash compatibility. Plus, for any indie game dev, to choose only one platform for your baby heavily limits its potential audience.

I have to admit now that the only thing I know about HTML5 is that it has a <canvas> tag. I know JavaScript, but not to its very fibers. I was mainly going as a deep-dive, and man, there were so many technologies that I have never heard before, and am bookmarking here for later investigation:

crafty.js
crafty.js is the Flixel of JavaScript. Collision detection! Sprite maps! Woo!

jsFiddle
The presentation for crafty.js had a lot of this jsFiddle stuff, which I guess is like a JavaScript console, which is super cool. I talked to Kelly about it excitedly and he was like, “Yeah. It’s pretty neat. I guess.” lol kelly whatever

Spaceport
Spaceport is another game engine which aids cross-platform support from web to iOS. Write your JavaScript and translate it to ActionScript like instantly! It has super good performance and will be in public beta sometime in September. You can purchase a license or, if you are a poor game dev, you can use Spaceport through a revshare model.

V8
During the Spaceport presentation, talk about something called V8 suddenly happened. It turns out that V8 is Google’s “open source, high performance JavaScript engine…written in C++ and used in Google Chrome.”

Google Web Toolkit (GWT)
The last speaker was a woman from Google who spoke briefly about GWT, which is a “Java software development framework” which allows you to build user interfaces, have client-server communication, etc. You can deploy applications you make to the Google App Engine.

PlayN
I couldn’t actually find a link for this yet, but PlayN is relevant to games on Google+.


There is just SO MUCH technology in the world. People didn’t really talk to each other during the meetup (then again, I came late and perhaps wasn’t trying hard enough to ~*network*~ with everyone), but still, being in a place like this really makes me feel inspired to learn more about everything.

Afterward, Kelly, Jonah and I walked through downtown talking about all the places we’ve been and the bros we had and went to Bi-Rite Creamery. I tried the Dainty Gentleman, which is a concoction of: 2 scoops of honey lavender ice cream, with drizzles of hot fudge and blood orange olive oil, sprinkled with seasalt. You would not believe the amazing flavors that comes out of this combination.

Adding a New Model to a Database in Rails

Aug 16th, 2011

For whatever reason, adding a model to a database in Rails took me forever to understand. Thanks to Kelly, I now have model-making in Rails down to an art.

Want to add a new table or a new column to your Rails application? This is what you do!


1. Create a new .rb file for your model

Let’s say that, just like me, you want to create a new database table in your application to keep track of information about dogs. We (by virtue of the same logic which affords physics problems friction-less vacuums with which to work in) already have a couple tables in our database: one pertaining to users (breeders or owners of dogs), and one pertaining to breeds. We will keep it simple for now; all we want to know about a dog object for now is what its name is, what user it belongs to, and what breed it is.

To add a Dogs table, we first need to create a file which will describe its model. Part of this is describing what the relationship of the new model is to existing models, like User and Breed. From real-world experience, we know that a dog generally has one user/owner, and one breed.

So, create a file in app/models/ called dog.rb, and type in the following:
class Dog < ActiveRecord::Base
  belongs_to :user
  belongs_to :breed
end
Dog inherits Active Record. Active Record’s sole purpose of existence is to help create associations between different objects. This code will later be macro’ed out by Active Record into something more lower-level to create the associations.

The lines that start with belongs_to describe the relationship that this new model has to existing ones. Basically, a dog belongs to one owner/user, and to one breed.

Rails also has another keyword called has_one, which at first might also seem like an okay association to make between dogs and users; after all, a dog has_one user, right? But belongs_to is more correct, because has_one implies more of a one-to-one relationship, where you might be doing something like creating a new user every time you create a new dog. Since users may later obtain more dogs, belongs_to would be more appropriate in the case you want to attach a new dog to an existing user. (Note: belongs_to also makes it so that the Dogs table will have to be responsible for its foreign key with Users, whereas it would be the opposite case for has_one.)


2. Change your existing models to reflect their relationship to the new model

Because a User can have many dogs, and have many different breeds of dogs, User should now look like this: 
class User < ActiveRecord::Base
  acts_as_authentic
  has_many :dogs
  has_many :breeds, :through => :dogs
end
And because many dogs can be of a certain breed, and breeds can be owned by multiple owners, Breeds will look the same – 
class Breed < ActiveRecord::Base
  has_many :dogs
  has_many :users, :through => :dogs
end


3. Generate a new migration

Now you have to create a migration which, when run, will create the relevant tables in a structured manner. You could also be hipster about it and edit SQL on your database by hand, but then you’d would have to tell the other people you’re working with of what you’ve done if they have local databases, or else run the same SQL on production databases. With migrations, you can make changes modular, and even roll back changes easily.

Run rails g migration create_dogs_table. This will create a file in db/migrate which will look something like this:
20110807213821_create_dogs_table.rb
The numbers on the left are a timestamp, which is needed by Active Record so it will know what migrations that you haven’t yet run, so that it can run them for you.



4. Edit the migration file to describe what tables or columns should be added to your database.

Open your migration file and type in these things: 
class CreateDogTable < ActiveRecord::Migration
  def self.up
  end

  def self.down
  end
end
self.up is executed when a database is updated; self.down is executed when a database is rolled back due to errors. So, in self.up you want to create your new tables and columns; in self.down you want to define code to remove those tables and columns. With dog objects, all we said we cared about earlier was the name, so let’s just make a table which will (automatically) contain a unique ID, a timestamp, and a string for each dog object. 
class CreateDog < ActiveRecord::Migration
  def self.up
    create_table :dogs do | d |
      d.timestamps
      d.string :name
    end
  end

  def self.down
    drop_table :dogs
  end
end


5. Run the migration to create your table

Run the migration by typing rake db:migrate. Remember how the migration that we generated had a timestamp? When you run rake db:migrate, Active Record can use those to figure out all the migrations that might have been added to your code base, which have not yet been run on a certain database (such as the one pertaining to production). Also, because the code to create and drop data from a database is so high-level, it doesn’t really matter whether your actual code is in sqlite3 or in MySQL. You can even use both depending on whether you’re in development or production stages.

Hurray! Now you have new tables and columns in your database!

Editing Skype Chat Messages

Aug 10th, 2011

There is one thing in particular that I wish Skype would change about its UI, and I think it’s mostly influenced by my experience using another 2.0-y bright blue user interface.

I’m talking about the little side menu that comes up over your chat messages, which offers you a variety of possible actions for that message.

man, I should have taken a bigger screenshot

When I am clicking on my own chat, these are the things that I probably want to do, in order:

  1. Edit whatever foolish, silly thing I just wrote
  2. Remove whatever now-irrelevant thing I just wrote

I can only imagine Copy or Quote to be useful in cases when I want to be snarky.  (I already said that!  Exactly 5 minutes and 3 seconds ago!) Or maybe if I’m cross-posting between chats?  I don’t know.  I accept there is a use case that I haven’t encountered for those options.

Nevertheless, what I really wish I could do in Skype is click anywhere on a chat-message of mine and have it open up into something I could immediately edit, much like Player.IO’s treatment of objects in its database.  The menu could then be reserved for the lesser use cases of Removing, Copying, or Quoting.

Speaking of the menu, could we also make that little arrow larger?  The little arrow that opens that menu in the first place?

do you see it?

Since there’s nothing usually relevant to the right side of the chat-message anyway, it would help a lot if the hitbox for the menu was larger.  Also, if the menu opened up automatically, instead of me having to click it…!

Just thinking about it is exciting. :D

Good Design Moves Tumblr Has Made Recently Which I Really Appreciate

Aug 8th, 2011

Tiled Photosets
If you wanted to post more than one picture at a time on Tumblr, it used to be that the pictures would be placed into a little slideshow applet. It was really elegant and cute; you would see one picture at a time, and could press a little rounded arrow to move on to the next picture.

The problem is, no one would use it. Or at least, if people did post this way, it didn’t get interacted with. (At least, I didn’t, and the rare frequency with which I met up with a photoset on my dashboard makes me think that others didn’t really like this method of posting either.) On Tumblr people just scroll, scroll, scroll. Occasionally laugh. And don’t stop for your dang photosets.

Then, suddenly! A tiled photoset appears!

with varied photo sizes, even!

All of a sudden, you can see all the pictures in a photoset at once! Plus the given layouts for different pictures make them all look really artsy, which Tumblr users love, because let’s be honest, over half of us are there just reblogging stuff. Though another mark of how cool this feature is is how people have taken creative liberties with it, making sets of images like this, or this, or even this.

Making a feature which fits with how people actually use the product, and allows them to be creative? ++!


Reblogging Tags
I actually just noticed this today. Never again will I have to repopulate the tags of something that I’m reblogging. As an example, textless.tumblr is a great blog for macro photography of insects (and other animals), and the photographer always dutifully tags all of his posts with whatever is pictured. Wooly Aphid. Larva. Wasp Mimic. Secropia Silkmoth. (These tags often even get cut off in the dashboard post. I can’t find an example now, but may later.) In any case, though I love the accuracy of it, no way am I going to spend any time tagging these reblogs with anything more than “photo” and maybe “bug.”

Fortunately, now tags also get reblogged! So, now reblogging things can make you feel even more productive with even less energy. :)


thanks, tumblr.  you the bomb
