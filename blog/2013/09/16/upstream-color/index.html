Upstream Color

Sep 16th, 2013

(source)
A man and woman are drawn together, entangled in the life cycle of an ageless organism. Identity becomes an illusion as they struggle to assemble the loose fragments of wrecked lives. (via)
[spoilers]

A and I watched Upstream Color this weekend, per the recommendations of various friends and coworkers. As someone on Reddit stated, one great thing about it is that the director does not underestimate the intelligence of his audience. As a viewer, I felt as if I was running to keep up —pausing at one scene with just enough time to grasp at understanding — before rushing on to the next — the next — the next.

A and I noted some scenes with awe at the “show, don’t tell” attention to detail. For example, at some point, the female protagonist says to her love interest, “Let’s buy a car,” and the next scene shows them driving an old vehicle with the price still painted on the windshield. I found the first “hyponosis” scene awesome for its strangeness, and yet so disturbing that I couldn’t bear to watch parts of the protagonist’s violation.

I loved that the medium so closely reflects the characters’ mental state; the choppiness of the timeline mirrors the difficulty that I imagine they must have had trying to reconstruct their lives after having been so compromised. The story also explores some facets (symptoms?) of the hypnotic state that I enjoyed; for example, once the main characters start telling stories to each other, they begin accepting them as their own, and begin arguing about whose memories actually belong to whom.

Upstream Color is great in its focus; it address the exploitation of hypnosis, and the repercussions of it on a human’s brain. (Will the victims ever be okay again? What does “okay” even look like anymore?) Upstream Color requires an intellectual athleticism that leaves you a little sore by the end, in a way that’s not unpleasant.
