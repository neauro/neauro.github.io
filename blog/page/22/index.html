Bridesmaids, and Stories With a Razor-sharp Focus and Relevant Characters

Jun 8th, 2011

(source)

In (somewhat premature) celebration of making it through the Computer Science department and retaining our artsyness, yesterday Jimmy and Mari and I went to Wann Izakaya for food and also saw Bridesmaids, which Jimmy wanted to see because the director apparently studied the art of story under another story guy that Jimmy really admires.

Something like that…

[spoilers ahead]

The movie was pretty funny. There were a ton of parts that were so awkward it was painful to watch (in a good way, I suppose). I didn’t mind to much while watching it, but afterward, talking about the story with Jimmy and Mari on the way home, it seemed to us that there was still a lot to be desired.

Jimmy for one is a huge proponent of focus in stories, and thinks that every movie should have an armature, which is essentially “the moral of the story.” The armature for The Godfather, for example, is that “Your family defines you.”

Throughout the entirety of Bridesmaids, the main character, Annie, appears to struggle with her negative outlook and lack self-confidence. She’s in a relationship with a clearly douche-y guy who doesn’t care about her, she struggles with another character who appears to be stealing her best friend away, she ends up pushing away the people who do care about her because of her own pessimism. Things begin to look up for her when she does decide to think positively, which occurs after her last, enormous mad outburst, and (apparently) due to one of her other “friends” tackling Annie on the couch and slapping her until Annie fought for herself.

If you’ve been depressed and thinking negatively for months, Jimmy pointed out, thinking positively doesn’t come easily. It takes a little more than a fat woman to beat you up on your furniture to make you think positively.

And once she does think positively, the solutions to all her problems just fell into her lap. Sure, she attempted and seemed to fail at apologizing to her love interest after wronging him – but that’s only a small step out of the comfort zone that she’s been hissing herself into the entire movie. It’s true that she never had to overcome anything greater than herself for the entire movie, but if doing so was so easy, why didn’t it just happen fifteen minutes in?

If a good movie should have focus, if every scene of it should mean something (much like how every sentence should mean something in a short story), and a big theme, then this movie fell something short of “good.” There seemed to be a lot of things that didn’t really mean anything – for instance, why was Annie a baker? The majority of the things that she did in that movie were completely unrelated to baking, and she only ever baked anything in two scenes. The first time that she baked something, it was after she met her love interest, and because he admitted he was a neighbor, I thought: Would she give it to her love interest? But no: she just looked at it, then ate it.

What is that supposed to mean? Does she bake to try and calm herself? If so, why doesn’t she do it the other times that she panics during the movie? Was she thinking about giving it to the love interest, and then was too afraid to do so?

Why was it important for Annie to be a baker? Was it just so that she could have a failed start-up, to show that she had ambition but had been broken by her failure? If so, couldn’t there have been a better way to do this, without giving her a completely superfluous label as “baker”? Or, if she definitely is a baker, why wouldn’t this show through in other aspects of her life? She doesn’t have recipe books. She doesn’t comment about the food that she eats. She doesn’t appear to have anything super baker-y in her room, which in a few shots is nice and elegant but completely lacking in description of the type of character that she is. People’s rooms can be the greatest indicator of what type of person they are – clean, messy, cluttered – my roommate’s typography decorations and shelves of comic books speak differently than my wall of folded-up paper chopstick-covers and small shelve stuffed with PS2 games. Mariko has a wire shelf of animu and Sakuracon badges. Jimmy has a triplet of monitors for his computer, Molly’s desk and windowsill is lined with classics and books about healthy eating.

As Jimmy texted, a couple minutes ago: Never have a batman movie that can also be a superman movie. Character must be intrinsic to plot. Annie could’ve been anything.

Which means, really, that she wasn’t anything.

There were a lot of other unnecessary parts – Annie’s mother maybe finding a new partner at the end, two of the bridesmaids becoming completely irrelevant at the end of the movie, so on. Anyway, it’s a funny movie.

Her Will (a Short Story for AU III: Legacy)

Jun 6th, 2011

AU!  woooo!

Last Wednesday was the third release party for AU, and also the last for this year, and also the last for its founding editors, who will all be graduating.

The party was small this time around, only about seven or so people, despite the advertisement of funfetti cookies and other handmade deliciousness. Apparently a ton of people submitted short stories for this quarter’s “Legacy” theme, and most of those stories were right on the border of 3,000 words. As AU’s size is apparently limited by how many pages the editors can physically staple together, they could only accept 6 submissions.

cover by katherine tung, once more

My submission is a short story titled Her Will, and you can find the full thing here.

When I heard about the theme, I searched Wikipedia and came to the conclusion that something playing off an inheritance or will would be a good idea. I also thought that it might be neat if a “will” were more literal – that is, that someone who died in some alternate universe would be able to possess some person to carry out a last task. Eventually this idea was trashed, but, meh. I may come back to it again.

I think that it’s fair to say I’ve never written short stories before AU; I just never really had the idea for it, nor the urge. I always found it hard to squeeze all my ideas into one short story. But I’ve discovered that writing short stories are surprisingly, uniquely fun – there’s something about it that’s almost like design. There has to be a meaning to every word, every sentence. For this story, I went beginning to end multiple times trying to cut out everything I couldn’t immediately justify, and strengthening everything else that was left. Though I would call the story far from perfect (when does any story ever reach that point?), even now I can’t think of one sentence that I would feel comfortable removing. I feared that some of the sentences would start to sound blunt and lifeless, but hopefully that’s not the case in the final draft.

I’m going to miss AU! Hopefully it will be taken up by new people next year, and I’ll be able to contribute since I’ll still be a UW (Master’s) student.

Get Across: Kongregate Release

May 23rd, 2011

the first 200-some players of our silly capstone game

It’s been about 24 hours since our first online release of Get Across. Our capstone class recommends moving from Newgrounds, to Kongregate, but then to Facebook, but it’s taken us such a long time to learn Player.IO that we skipped straight to Kongregate, as Player.IO already supports this..

Still, our release was (to me) a little rushed, and I would have loved nothing better than to clutch the game even tighter and keep anyone from seeing it at all. We have presentations on Tuesday on what our first round of releasing reveals to us about the game, and I haven’t parsed through the data that we’ve collected yet, but an onslaught of a mysterious error regarding null quest objects made me really self-conscious about what was going on. I don’t want anyone to play a game that is still buggy. Nononononono!

It’s hard to debug from Player.IO’s error log, though it is very informative concerning what errors popped up, whether they were serverside or clientside, when they happened, and how often.

just look at those graphs, dude!

Some of the players on Kongregate have been really helpful in leaving comments about their thoughts and the bugs they’ve encountered; but there are about 6 of these comments, and 200+ registered players, most of whom didn’t really make it through the tutorial levels. I have a good guess why: the tutorials take forever to load.

I’m not sure why, but I’m guessing it might be because we have a lot of objects in the database, and a lot of the properties of those objects are nested objects or arrays, which must take longer to retrieve. Seeing as tutorial levels are pretty essential for hooking in initial players, I’m hoping to make it so that the tutorial levels are completely static and client-side – no saving data into the database, nothing. If a player comes back, they’ll just have to start over. Hopefully this will make it so that we only really save “real” player data in the database as well, and keep out the clutter.

So even though I really wouldn’t have wanted to release the game this soon, this little bit of playtesting illuminated something that we otherwise would have learned at the last awful minute, for the final Facebook release. Which is nice.

As a sidenote, the last push I made to Get Across before the release were updated character sprites for our classes, “Cook,” “Crafter,” and “Planter.” Charlie drew the first set, which were endearingly derpy –


And these are the more recent ones –


Hopefully more character customization will come soon, though it’s hard to say what we’ll have time for. 2 weeks left to polish up the back-end code and the mechanics!

Player.IO – Thoughts on the BigDB Administration UI

May 8th, 2011

I’m in Intro to Human Centered Interaction this quarter and so can’t help being a little more thoughtful about the way things are designed, especially since I’m trying to get better at designing myself. That being said, I’ve also been working with Player.IO all weekend, in particular the database-side stuff, and have some good-natured criticisms.

Firstly – the Player.IO administration area is pretty. :D

is this really all for me? <3

At a glance, everything looks very orderly and uncluttered. Not only that, but the things you presumably want to do when you look at this page – creating a table, adding a new object to a table, etc. – are all very apparent. Player.IO doesn’t need to provide an intro tutorial for how to use (most of) their UI because the functions it offers are obvious.

The layout also relies a lot on symbols to communicate ideas, i.e. the green “plus sign” for adding an object or adding a property to an object, or the red “minus sign” for deleting an object. Luckily, there’s alt text to explain what each symbol does, as I can imagine someone could get confused by the difference between “adding an object” or “adding a property to an object,” especially as they both use some variation of the green plus sign.

why did this computer not take a screencap of the cursor hovering over the green plus sign?
the world may never know

When you want to change an object’s property, you just click it. There’s even a helpful “click to edit” text which shows whenever your cursor hovers over a property.

it’s easy!

Once you click a property, you click “Okay” and you’re all done!

…except, you’re not. o__o One of the first things I had to get used to was the “Save Changes” button, which is what you need to press in order to finalize the changes that you’ve made to an object.

aaack

The main reason why this is so easy to forget is because, other than the appearance of the button, there’s no real visible indication that an object is “dirty” or has been altered and needs to be saved; and once you click “Save Changes,” the only indication that you have of success is the “Save Changes” button disappearing, which just doesn’t feel affirming. It’s like – were the changes saved? Did they fail? Can I leave now?

Saving your data is so subtle and anti-climatic that it’s easy to forget to do it.

wait – wh – nooooooooo

Then again, maybe the “Save Changes” button is the way it is because input fields to change properties opens whenever you click on a property, whether you actually wanted to change that property or not. The only way to close out of changing a property once this happens is to say either say “okay” or “Delete.” That being said, putting more prominence in the form of a confirmation message to the “Save Changes” button would probably make more sense than making the “Okay” button a “Cancel” button to close input-listening.

even if you accidentally open this input field,
“Okay” and “Delete” make more sense
than “Cancel” and “Delete,” i suppose

A related issue: if you happen to click on an object or an array accidentally, that object or array gets emptied of all its data. Since no alteration you make to a property gets saved until you press “Save Changes,” at least it’s not a permanent emptiness; but, there’s no way to undo this object/array-emptying, so to undo you need to refresh the object completely. This can be frustrating if you’ve made a lot of changes to other properties in the object, and now need to cancel all those changes just to get your emptied-out object/array back.

Speaking of arrays, it took me forever to figure out how to add an array property to an object. It turns out that this is the proper way to do it:

  1. In your object, press “+” to create a new property.
  2. Set the new property’s value to “Value is: Array.”
  3. Press “Okay.”
  4. You’ll see something like the image above. Click “Show array.”
  5. Add properties to the array one by one by clicking “+”.

This is a pretty roundabout method, considering that to make a value of a property an int, all you need to do is put an int in the field, then say “Okay.” When trying to put in an array, I was expecting to somehow form my input so that the values would get fed into the array automatically.


It’s also annoying that you have to click “Show array” before adding properties to it; it would be nice if the entire array just popped open when you went to add a property to it, since that’s probably what you’re going to do if you’re clicking an array in the first place. Clicking anywhere in that row outside of “show array” will just bring you back to the weird input box right above “Value is: Array.” This is one case in which the “+” button for adding properties is unhelpfully small. (And of course, if you miss it and accidentally click somewhere, your entire array gets emptied, as I mentioned above.)

Anyway, when I was making this example, I noticed Player.IO does a good job of telling you when your fields are malformed. It might be marginally nicer to put some error message like, “This field needs an int in it, because that’s what you said duh” but I don’t know; maybe that would mess up the minimalism. Then again, minimalist aesthetic should be about only giving you the information you need, right?


Another miscellaneous issue: you can’t do batch alterations/deletions of objects. When I go through and cleanse all my test accounts and related information, I have to delete every single object by hand, rather than just selecting them all and deleting them in a fell swoop. Time-consuming and annoying.

All this being said, working with Player.IO has still been tons of fun. I would have had no idea how to implement all this back-end stuff by myself – or at the very least, it would have taken me much longer to figure out how to do.

I JUST WANT MY CITY to BE a CITY THAT I WOULD WANT to LIVE In

May 7th, 2011


This is the new downtown area that I tried to clear up. Before it was just a dense cluster of Country Homes, which are the first tier of housing that you can buy, and thus is the building that is presently the most useless to me. I need more population in less space! But something about bulldozing houses for Milan Apartments or Upscale Condos is a little uncomfortable.

My most recent additions to the city of Tummy are a cheese store and a candle shop, which are named Tummy and Whey, and The Tummy At Both Ends, respectively.

Anyway.

Last week or so I attended the first Seattle [Game Dev] Indies meetup at The Capitol Club, which I walked past twice before actually discovering. Though I cannot comfortably call myself an “indie” game dev (unless you count my research projects and the burgeoning Get Across), I wasn’t quite sure what my place was, but in general just wanted to learn more about the community and see what new experiences I could make. Everyone was nice (though some were understandably horrified that I have not yet played Portal 2, nay, have not even played the first Portal), even Dan for some reason remembered me from my stint helping him take surveys for his game two years ago, and I was super happy to run into Alex again. I learned about the tragedy of Infiniminer, that a lot of people are making abstract music games, that Flixel is much more widely used than I thought, that Player.IO really is the way to go for multiplayer Flash games, which is good, because we finally finished paper prototyping stage and started coding Get Across.

you the bestest

Working with Player.IO has been really simple and fun, though it’s taking a while for me to wrap my head around noSQL and the whole “We don’t have rows, we have objects!” thing. I want to learn more about noSQL. Is it faster than relational databases? IS it still a relational databases?! Somehow an initial Google search was fruitless.

Some advantages of Player.IO:
  • I would have had no idea how to implement network stuff otherwise, and the given code and examples make it relatively easy
  • GUI is really nice!
  • I can view every item in my database and alter each one?  Wat!
  • Database queries are all asynchronous, which makes them fast

And some problems we’ve run into:
  • There are three people in our team, and when we are all working together, one of us will sometimes hang at the “joining a room” dialogue
  • Running into vague “InternalError” problems when loading/updating the database is a pain…nothing to do for it but wait until something works all of a sudden :/
  • Database queries are all asynchronous, which makes it so I get results back at weird/wrong times

Not deal-breakers overall, and I’m getting more and excited. GET ACROSS GO

Sakura-Con 2011

Apr 26th, 2011




Edit 11/29: Links to photo galleries can be found in this post!

This is a video Timmy found on Youtube by FastMatt which is essentially the only proof (1:27) that I have of cosplaying at this year’s Con. (Actually, there are a couple pictures, but I’m kind of dissatisfied with them, and didn’t really have a chance to take pictures of myself…) I did Glaceon and – oh gosh, I just realized I left my costume at the Convention Center…

….

…well, it wasn’t that hard to make. >____>;; It was basically a light blue kimono and some darker portions made with darker blue, all in this sort of quilt fabric that I found at JoAnn’s. (Quilt fabric is always in much more versatile colors…) Since I’ve made gi/kimono-style clothings at least twice before for Con purposes I pretty much just haphazardly threw it together, yet I feel that it’s my most successful cosplay yet. Probably because I was with all the other Eevees, and I think we made up a pretty impressive gijinka group. :3

this is kind of an unflattering picture of me, but a good one of everyone else :D

We had more pro pictures taken by Rachel, an apparently amazing artist and cosplayer from San Francisco who does photography as a hobby. She’s uber busy though, so it will probably take a couple months for us to actually get the pictures that she took of us. (Apparently she’s putting off photo processing until early June! Just enough time to forget about them and then be happy to see them later. :D) We scrambled over the stone features of the Washington State Convention Center and afterwards shivered in turns as we waited for Eric to finish taking individual shots of all of us.

this is a pic that rachel took of some other of merch staff cosplaying blazblue at last year’s con
…for the sake of having a picture here

I was busier at this Con than any other year before, mostly because of commitments as staff, and a lesser willingness to get up early and take advantage of wandering Con in the morning. FFXIII and Vocaloid cosplays seemed to be especially popular, or at least, that’s what I remember the most, aside from the usual smattering of Akatsuki cloaks, and an interesting blend of different video games, including Minecraft, haha. Also, tons of Hetalia.

My favorite thing to do at Con is usually take pictures, but this year I found I wasn’t quite so interested in wandering and asking cosplayers for their pictures…maybe because I’ve become too jaded, or maybe because I’m just tired of the process itself. For me the joy of Con now comes mostly just from submerging myself in the atmosphere of it – the excited fanpeoples, the cosplayers who have worked so hard, the shy people who come out all smooth and soft-spoken from their quiet shells from all corners of Seattle and beyond.

It used to be that I would also go for all the stuff you could buy, but I guess I’ve grown out of that too – just – can’t think of anything to buy. That being said, I never used to enjoy Artist Alley, but I think I’ve finally decided that it’s another one of my favorite parts of Con – being there has such a clean, nice, inspiring feeling, unlike being in the general dealer’s room. I bought a beautiful print from Zilva and a poster for Andou from Han Tseng, from Directions of Destiny (which I started reading soooo long ago! Guess it’s time to check up on it again). I actually grabbed the poster on Sunday; I went back home for Easter celebrations and then rushed from the terminal to fifth floor, where I located Han Tseng’s booth, already 90% taken down…luckily he was nice enough to pull out a poster for me despite.

Also this year at Con, I had my picture taken by a DSi for the first time. I wonder what I would have thought of such a thing back when I started attending in 2006…that handhelds and cellphones have become common cameras is just crazy.

So, in summary: Con was great! Can’t wait for the next one. :D
