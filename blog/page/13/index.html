Glow, Amy Kathleen Ryan

Jul 15th, 2012

(source)

I rarely do book reviews, because I personally feel that what makes a book good is a convoluted stew of good feelings and fresh learning that can’t be condensed into recipe. Furthermore, when I encounter a book I dislike, I concede that there are many people who like it, who were searching within it for something that I was not searching for, something that they found and I didn’t. Why look for the sky in the sea?

I want to ~*engage more critically*~ with what I read, however, so…here I go!

First, the cover description:
The Empyrean is the only home fifteen-year-old Waverly has ever known. Part of the first generation to be successfully conceived in deep space, she and her boyfriend, Kieran, will be pioneers of New Earth. Waverly knows she must marry young in order to have children who can carry on the mission, and Kieran, the handsome captain-to-be, has everything Waverly could want in a husband. Everyone is sure he’s the best choice. Still, there’s a part of Waverly that wants more from life than marriage, and she is secretly intrigued by the sky, darkly brilliant Seth.

Suddenly, Waverly’s dreams are interrupted by the inconceivable – a violent betrayal by the Empyrean’s sister ship, the New Horizon. The New Horizon’s leaders are desperate to populate the new planet first, and will do anything to get what they need: young girls. In one pivotal moment, Waverly and Kieran are separated, and they find themselves at the helms of dangerous missions, where every move has potentially devastating consequences, and the decisions of the heart may lead to disaster.
I found this book on the library shelf and thought the premise was interesting enough. I would have said maybe half a year ago that I adore YA, but recently most YA novels have disappointed me in terms of the shallowness with which they engage their issues, and inability to stray from a plot that involves a love triangle.

Glow is the first book of a YA trilogy, because YA novels always come in triplets now, I suppose. The story is so fast-paced that there’s not enough time to learn about anyone. What you do know about people is told to you outright, rather than shown firsthand through character interactions, through things that happen.

One of the first characters you are told about is Waverly, through the eyes of Kieran.
[Waverly] was a watchful person, and intelligent, a trait that showed in the deliberate way her dark eyes found their mark and held it steady. She had a way of seeing into people and understanding their motives that Kieran found almost unnerving […]
This description comes right after Kieran asks Waverly to marry him, and comes in lieu of Waverly actually doing any of the things that Kieran says are characteristic of her. When Waverly guesses earlier that Kieran is extending a proposal to her, it isn’t written like something that she understands with her Waverly-special power. At some point Waverly is “unreadable in the way she looked at him, waiting, saying nothing,” which could parallel the way “her dark eyes found their mark and held it steady” – in which case, why not put Kieran’s observation of her characteristic gaze there, right when the action is happening?

Another character is the Captain:
In truth, the Captain was the most purposeful man on the ship, quick with his decisions, which were almost always right, and trusted by all the men on the ship, though he was less popular with women.
Imagine how much more compelling the Captain character would have been if he was shown in some brief situation with his council or something, and the Captain said, “Listen, this thing just happened, so we need to do X,” and the men said, “Oh my gosh, I must act on your will immediately,” whereas the women eyed him sidelong and were like, “I don’t know about this.” You would have learned that the captain was “trusted by all the men on the ship, though he was less popular with the women,” with the added bonus of actually seeing it yourself.

This is assuming that it’s ever going to be important that the Captain is less popular with women, which it isn’t. On page 82 it turns out that Waverly has felt uncomfortable in the male gaze of many men on the ship, which means that the fact that the Captain is unpopular as an individual should be unworthy of note. It just doesn’t mean anything in the long run.

Backstories are also blurted out in solid paragraphs. You’re handheld through Waverly’s history with Seth, her second love interest, and with her ex-best-friend Felicity. Maybe the point of the book wasn’t for Waverly share these experiences firsthand with the reader; but the resulting effect is that these characters are like dolls that are handed to you, rather than “real people.”

There are also characters that are completely meaningless, like Serafina Mbewe, “a sweet little girl” who is deaf. What does it mean that she’s deaf? Does deafness happen all the time? Did this ship heading for the brand new human world prepare for deaf people? Can Serafina communicate at all? Later, after an announcement by Anne Mathers that the girls’ families are dead, Waverly “picked up Serafina Mbewe, who was crying” – wait, what? Did deaf Serafina hear the announcement? Lipread it? Did Serafina just pick up the distressed atmosphere of the girls around her? Is Serafina just meant to be the token helpless child that shows up to prove that Waverly cares about other people, even if Waverly doesn’t exhibit any caring by way of worrying about her or thinking about her throughout the book? Serafina is a deaf child amidst a world of not-deaf children, so she sticks out; but she does little to support Waverly’s character, and doesn’t do anything to advance the plot.

The book’s premise necessarily involves issues of gender and sex, but the way it treats it is shallow, and in some places cringe-worthy. In a book with so many female characters, it’s still all about women reacting to men’s actions and desires. The women of the book are notable for wanting children. And here’s Waverly, on page 93:
Days before, her biggest worry was about marrying Kieran. She should have said yes to him, with no hesitation. Yes, Kieran, I will marry you. I love you. He needed to hear that, and she should have given it to him.
Wait, WHAT?! Waverly – what about your feelings? Are we seriously reading about something like marriage being something that Waverly should have done to please Kieran? Maybe this is supposed to be an example of Waverly succumbing to men’s desires, you know, a bad example, something that she rises above – but, nope. Waverly muses on how Kieran deserved her approval and acceptance of him, despite any (unsaid) feelings she has about him herself, and that’s that.

There are also issues with setting. Waverly has been on a spaceship her entire life. At some point, Waverly describes:
Few adults on the Empyrean had been willing to talk about their planet of origin…. They always changed the subject if she asked too many questions […]
Which means that what Waverly knows about Earth, or Earth things, likely comes only from the ship’s environment, or from the classes she’s taken. Yet, later:
“Have you ever had ice cream?”

“We don’t carry cows on the Empyrean,” Waverly said […]
Wait, what? Okay, so there are no cows on the Empyrean – but Waverly knows what a cow is? Even if she had learned about what a cow is, through classes or through an adult’s hidden past, Waverly not only knows what a cow is, but that milk can be harvested from it, and that milk can be processed to create food products, which includes something called ice cream? Waverly knows all this about a creature which she has never seen personally, and who she really should have no reason to even learn about, since there are no cows on the Empyrean?

It’s an example of the narrowness of this setting despite its uniqueness. How do young characters like Waverly, born on the ship, clash with characters that once lived on Earth? Was the transition from “I have an entire world to roam” to “Now I live on this ship” made with such ease that no child ever sees an adult struggle with it? (I mean, older people seem to struggle all the time with the ease with which children master iPods and computers.) Is there even amber on the Empyrean for Waverly to use as reference when she describes Kieran’s eyes as “amber brown”? Even if there was, wouldn’t it be so much cooler if Waverly was like, Wow, Kieran’s eyes are amber, the color of something that exists on my home planet that I’ve never seen, which can only be made from the amazing phenomenon of placing tree resin under sustained heat and pressure?

Furthermore, certain problems that I expected don’t crop up at all; for example, didn’t anyone on this mission ever think about inbreeding? They want to populate an entire new world of humans, yet impregnate dozens of women from the eggs of a couple girls? There’s not even one note about how bad this could potentially be in the distant future?

What I did like about this book was the movement of religion through the characters, and especially the switch-around that happened between the ships and Waverly and Kieran’s motivations. Given the pace and breadth of characters and setting introduced, however, “Glow” really feels like the second of its trilogy, a book that hands over its characters and settings as given, rather than letting them grow.

Watched Brave the Other Day,

Jul 11th, 2012

and was a little disappointed by it. (spoilers ahead)

the art was gorgeous, though (source)

The movie on the whole is beautiful, but the characters and story felt shallow and unfocused. Soon after the start of the movie, Merida describes her family and lifestyle, from the fact that her mother is controlling to the existence of her three younger brothers – a huge contrast from introductory sequences a la WALL-E and Up, where you are given the chance to watch characters interact, learn what they do and what they love without anyone explaining it to you like a textbook.

Throughout the movie there were little actions that I wish had had more background or more follow-through, more meaning in general.

For example, when her mother gave her that necklace – how important was it? Wouldn’t it have felt so much more painful to watch Merida give that necklace away to the witch if you had known anything about it? If it was something her mother had inherited, something that stood for all the tradition and love that her mother was trying to give to her, which Merida then selfishly threw away?

And, why is it so important for Merida to be introduced as an amazing archer – what purpose did her archery ever serve to the story? There’s the competition scene, but her shooting in that case didn’t make any difference to the fact she was still going to be married off. For the rest of the movie, whenever she shoots an arrow (like at that Big Evil Magic Bear) it doesn’t do anything. Contrast to Katniss in The Hunger Games, whose proficient archery is used at all times to express her character: it’s used in her illicit yet altruistic hunting activity, it’s what she uses to defy the Capitol during her evaluation, it’s what she uses to explode her enemies’ supplies before she’s ready to actually kill them, and it’s what she uses when she does kill her first human being. In fact, at the end of Brave you saw Merida showing off more of her sword skills when she prevents her father from attacking her mother. So, why stress her archery?

The movie also worked off some assumptions of marriage that I wish had been explained. Like – why is Merida so immediately opposed to marriage? Why does marriage even need to happen in this story, other than the fact that it’s “The Tradition”? At some point Merida’s mother starts lecturing off this story about how Tradition is Important, but it still doesn’t have any tie-in to why Merida has to be married, or why this is such an oppressive thing. I mean, her mother is doing okay, right?  Her mother is shown to wield an incredible amount of respect among all the other men in the movie, walking among their brawls without a scratch, pacifying them with ear pinches.

And the way this “I don’t want to be married!” conflict ended made me cringe. Essentially, Merida confronts the other clan leaders and their sons and, with the blessing of her (pantomiming, bear-shaped) mother, declares “It’s time for us to break away from tradition!”

And the men go, “Hmm, okay!”

It was just so….aww, man. Because, assuming marriage is an institution of oppression towards women (which it’s treated as during this movie), it’s dissolved way too easily – essentially by women (Merida, and her mother) deciding for themselves that they no longer want to be held beneath it. It reeks of the idea that people choose whether or not to be a victim – as if this whole time, Merida and her mother had just been “oppressing themselves.” Brave is a fictional children’s movie, but the idea that “People can just get whatever they want if they just ask for it” is one that’s very well-believed, and in the end I would have preferred to see Merida really fight to “change her fate,” as the move’s tagline stated, instead of “discover that the true power to succeed was within her all along,” which is blatantly American Dream-y.

Really fight someone else other than her mother, I mean, who was I guess is the only person in this movie not progressive enough to be okay with Merida not marrying.

Even if you don’t want to think about it from a social justice-y standpoint, it’s still mind-boggling why the problem Merida had in this movie was marriage. Merida’s mother wanted marriage for the purposes of “keeping peace” between the clans; but the clan-leaders and their sons clearly didn’t want marriage badly enough to argue when Merida proposed an alternative.

The last thing I want to mention: what was up with that magical warrior bear? I guess it was chasing Merida at the very beginning of the movie, and at the end, surrounded by people, was only interested in mauling her?   The way that he dissolved into blue at the end seemed to imply that he had some relation to the will-o-the-wisps, but I don’t get how Merida was important to him at all.

That all being said, I guess I’m still looking forward to Pixar’s next movie.

Industry of Nature: Another Approach to Ecology

Jun 22nd, 2012


I found Industry of Nature: Another Approach to Ecology at one of the front tables in the library and could not resist its nicely designed print pages. The book is basically a series of animals and evolutionary characteristics, followed by a small “implications for design” section.

For example, the book points out that sharks rarely have barnacles and bacteria on their skin, something that other fish and whales often have. This is because sharkskin is too rough and prickly to support such creatures easily, and this inspired the manufacture of a texture which could be stuck to things like doorknobs, and reduced the number of bacteria growing there purely by texture alone. Whoa.

Another part of the book talked about how the pattern made by fallen leaves was always beautiful. I read it once again, thinking, Wait, really? What the hell could this inspire design-wise, and lo, it actually inspired carpet which could look good no matter what direction its tiles were placed in. I guess some patterned carpet needs to get laid in a certain order in order to look right, and there’s some 17% cut-off in such carpet when the laying-down goes awry – but with randomly-patterned carpet, there was only 2% cut-off! Wat!

There were many more creatures and plants that inspired different awesome designs, and it reminded me of that kid who looked at trees and then got a patent for his solar panels arrays, which were arranged in a spiral like the branches of a tree and were able to collect 20% or more energy than normal panels.  Damn nature, u smart though.

I hope I’m not missing out on too much nature intelligence by keeping my head in books like this all the time.

A Deluge of Recent-er News, and Some Free-to-play Chat

Jun 22nd, 2012

What have I been up to recently? Namely:

vim, and the shiba inu puppy cam

My first larger-scale game project drew to an “official” close last Friday (“official” meaning “It’s actually going to be debugged and stuff for the next couple weeks, but for sanity purposes, it’s ‘done’”). Also last Friday was the end of spring quarter, in which I took a class about empirical research and learned some fundamental but important things like, qualitative research is not meant to be judged on its external validity, but rather it’s transferability. The former is the extent to which findings can be “generalized” to the broader world; the latter is the extent to which findings may be found under certain limitations and settings, which have to be defined by the researchers. Subtle.

I’ve also been playing a lot of Diablo 3, despite my deep discomfort with the fact that the only playable dark-skinned character is a witch doctor. Like – really? Witch doctor? The only playable black person is a spasming, exotified mush of skull masks and voodoo-flavored equips? When this is brought up in conversation circles the immediately following statement always seems to be, But, but the (spoilers) fallen angel of justice is black, as if this suddenly makes witch doctor okay, and I guess it’s soothing if what’s important is that the games you play be falsely “perfect” enough to not be worth critical analysis.

That being said, I’m still enjoying my social slot machine pinata game Diablo, though the amount of enjoyment dims a bit into grimacing cynicism every time I find an article about its psychological trickery, like this one.

Since it came out recently, and since my next project (and conceivably all future projects from now on) involve free-to-play games, I’ve also been playing Nimblebit’s recently released Pocket Planes to see what I can glean from its charming pixelated ailerons.  In Pocket Planes, you construct your very own airline empire, overseeing the passage of travelers and unusual cargo from city to city.

my plane, named “faire,” since the colors seemed kinda fair-like

In particular I was struggling with how I could make an “ecosystem” of goals, something that wouldn’t require an endless content feed or “leveling up,” some system that could provide its player prodding naturally and grow without much goading and prodding on my part.  Something that’s nice about Pocket Planes is that, whenever you view the map, you see your “goals:” all those faded-out cities.


seoul, i will get you soon enough

I recently tried Kairosoft’s recent Epic Astro Story as well, and it had the same sort of carrot-dangling – faded out areas with rocket ships and caves waiting to explore. In some ways I feel like it’s more fun to decide where you want to expand into as well, given the little bit of information.  Maybe it’s also ~*intrinsic*~, since you know that you’re getting something when you expand, but there’s still a surprise and mystery concerning what you can do with it.

screenshot of epic astro story, lite version

Something that I like about both games is that they have warm little cores of personalization/personality. In Pocket Planes, you can color your planes how you like, you can name them, you can outfit their pilots so they have unexpectedly bright facial hair, you can bring the Rosetta Stone from Nagasaki to Sendai, you can bring an ambiguously George Washington-dressed person to Vladivostok.  In Epic Astro Story, you have individual citizens with their own names and their own households and preferences.  It’s the sort of thing that I’m having a hard time finding a parallel for for the amusement park simulation, since amusement parks don’t really have little fictional people with natural needs (like work/leisure/exploration) to inhabit them.

Recently one of my coworkers admitted that he had never been to Disneyland and was going in the near future, and as soon as people found out they’ve been taking time to cross vast distances to reach his desk, after which some thirty minutes to an hour will be spent bestowing upon said coworker nostalgic stories and adamant recommendations about Disneyland.  ”I remember the first time I did X, I felt so [scared/excited/happy]…it’s been years now but I really want to go back!” I wish I could capture this sense of excitement, this sort of exuberant memory essence for this amusement park game, but I haven’t yet been able to come up with much except perhaps randomly generated events where a little fake visitor will say, “I remember visiting this Fun Bounce in my youth, I love it!”

I kind of wish I could visit Disneyland again just to see if inspiration would strike.

Things I Learned From Extra Virginity by Tom Mueller

May 30th, 2012

the cover of extra virginity, tom mueller (source)

  1. Good olive oil can be bitter and pungent and it is likely that I have never tasted it before, ever, due to my reliance on bulk supermarket oils, some of it even taken home in plastic bottles (apparently an obscenity, as olive oil, unlike wine, gets worse with age, and does not keep well in plastic, which can allow exterior scents and stuff to get into the oil).
  2. Olive oil that is not bitter and pungent is likely to not have many of the Good Healthy Things olive oil has in it, due to it having been refined such that all the Good Healthy Things fled away in horror.
  3. These Good Healthy Things include antioxidants, which fight against oxidation, which is vital for biological systems but also the cause of cell damage.
  4. Olive oil contains a variety of fatty acids, which are unsaturated fats, meaning their their molecular bonds are not all connected to hydrogen atoms, and thus kinda bendy, and open to “invasion” by oxygen, which can react with the molecule and cause rancidity. In contrast, a saturated fat has its connections rounded out by hydrogens and has a tight crystal structure which is less prone to oxygen invasion.  For this reason, some foods’ fats are subjected to a process which forces hydrogen into the molecules, so that it has longer shelf life.  But this means the food gains what is called hydrogenated fat or trans fat, and if there’s any health professional in the world that says that this is safe for you to put in your body in any quantity, it’s not written in this book.
  5. The modern-day “extra virgin olive oil” should indicate that the oil has been created by olives that have yielded their juice through a centrifuge, and not undergone any refining treatment (which can involve deodorizing or bleaching).  The term “extra virgin olive oil” doesn’t mean a lot, however, since the majority of olive oil sold by companies is labeled “extra virgin,” and may still be partially refined or cut with other vegetable or seed oils.
After reading this book I wanted desperately to try a Real Olive Oil, but the book mostly recommends audiences to go straight to the mills and purchase there, and no amount of googling for Real Olive Oils accessible in the Pacific Northwest could refute this.  Maybe I’ll be able to try one out next time I’m in California.

I loove learning the names of plants, especially of flowers, especially when a flower turns out to be one whose name I had all along, unhashed to a visual.  For example, camellias.

you know, those ones that rot so quickly & become thick pink mud

My super-pro flower-identifying technique goes like this: take a picture.  Describe flower to Google Images.  Check tags.

purple flower cluster tree…small purple flowers tall…small purple flowers tall bush…

Aha!  Looks like it may be the common purple lilac?!

“common” makes it feel less cool somehow

I’ve been seeing a lot of bell-shaped flowers recently in varying purples, pinks, and whites.  It’s shocking how many results you can get from purple bell-shaped flower.  I was mixed for a long time and couldn’t decide whether these are bluebells or wood hyacinths.  General bluebells look quite different, but wood hyacinths and specific spanish bluebells look very similar.

Edit: WELP, I guess spanish bluebells and wood hyacinths are actually the same type of flower, and potentially invasive in the Pacific Northwest.

ye noxious weed!

Here’s another plant for which a search for small white flower clusters yields many possible results.  Is it spirea (the razor-edged leaves seem similar, but not the long stamens)?  Or is it a type of viburnum (which have such a variety that I’m not sure whether this flower is one of them or not)?  I’m thinking viburnum, but I guess I’ll have to wait and see whether the plant ever bears red/black fruit, as viburnum apparently does.

The Women of Avacyn Restored

Apr 28th, 2012

I’ve been kind of annoyed with the women that are featured on Magic’s creatures before; in general, male characters have a ton of diversity (with representations as Rakish Heir, Thraben Doomsayer, Disciple of Griselbrand), whereas women tend to be like…Crossway Vampire, or even Morkrut Banshee, with only half a face to speak of but wearing a corset which features her perky boobs.

Imagery like this says: in Magic World, men can be a variety of things, they can be young or old or fat or wrinkled, but if women are there, she better be supple and breastful, like, maybe something that a guy can look at while his opponent is thinking out their turn.

Part of the reason why I started getting into Magic during the Innistrad block is because I loved the women werewolves and the fact they could become enormous unsexualized female creatures.  The release of Avacyn Restored, the final set of Innistrad, came with a ton of other fun female(-assigned?) creatures, like…


Farbog Explorer (source)

Farbog Explorer: Look at you.  Look at your hat.  You are awesome.


Sigarda, Host of Herons (source)
Sigarda, Host of Herons: who shows more variety to angelic armor than what’s shown on other angels like Herald of War the Angel token, who have that odd cleavage-bearing armor that most fantasy characters have.  In general, her femininity is also less pronounced than other Angel creatures.

Bruna, Light of Alabaster (source)
Speaking of angels, there’s Bruna, Light of Alabaster, who (despite potentially flashing everyone that she flies over), is a darker-skinned angel, which is pretty neat.

Cathedral Sanctifier (source)
Speaking of darker-skinned characters, Cathedral Sanctifier is also pretty neat – though her skin still strikes me as pretty light, which I mention still due to the privilege that lighter-skinned PoC have over darker-skinned PoC, as the movie Dark Girls points out in clarity.  Other cool priest-y creatures: Holy Justiciar, Devout Chaplain.

Thraben Valiant (source)
Thraben Valiant: Awesome armor, awesome impossibly-curved whip-thing, awesome hat.

Riders of Gavony (source)
Riders of Gavony: It’s a female human knight!  Go go go!

Kruin Striker (source)
Kruin Striker: I guess she wants to kill the wolfir, which is a little sad for me story-wise, since I like them…but I also appreciate that this provides more cool “women who have agendas!” flavor.  She also helped me in most of my pre-release matches, so, totally legit.

Elgaud Shieldmate (source)
Elgaud Shieldmate: So cool.  I only regret being unable to play her with Latch Seeker during pre-release.

Gryff Vanguard (source)
Gryff Vanguard: Another sweet human knight, plus she’s riding a cool heron-gryphon thing, so.

Champion of Lambholt (source)
Champion of Lambholt: Again, cool for adding to the variety of looks/things in Innistrad, and also for potentially overcoming the multitude of spirit tokens in the metagame, haha.


Lingering “Oh Well :/” Things:

  • The majority of creatures in Innistrad are still white.  I mean, I guess the entire set is inspired from European fantasies, and traditional Europe is white-skinned, but I just find it mind-boggling that it’s easier to imagine angels and demons living in a fictional world than it is to imagine darker-skinned humans.  It’s also frustrating to continually see high fantasy scenarios the realm of mostly-white characters.  Would the world-building really suffer for having more PoC there?  Would people get up in arms about it?  If so, consider that players would be arguing that they want their fictional worlds to be mostly white, and then realize what this means for semi-subconscious racism.
  • Even though there are more women and more types of women filling different roles in Innistrad, I would consider them all still conventionally beautiful in their thinness and youth.  Contrast, again, with the male characters: Diregraf Escort, Silverblade Paladin, and Timberland Guide (all of whom I consider middle-aged-looking), Nightshade Peddler (not conventionally beautiful, period), Alchemist’s Apprentice (which just makes me realize that I haven’t yet noticed a close-up of a female character that just shows her face, you know, minus boobs or identifying skirt), Nephalia Smuggler (who is clearly not thin)…and so on.
  • Both of the wolfir cards (Wolfir Silverheart and Wolfir Avenger) look pretty masculine to me, though CML thinks they look pretty androgynous.  The wolfir is what happened when Avacyn repressed the werewolf curse in certain humans, so given the density of female werewolves, I was a little disappointed there weren’t more female-looking wolfir…but in my mind perhaps they chose to stay as werewolves and live far away and free :)
  • The card Triumph of Ferocity, which somehow got printed despite depicting sexualized violence.  Like, really?  Aren’t these cards vetted?  Why did a variety of designers/developers/artists think it was okay to depict a man pinning a woman by her throat, raising a blade above her, with his knee between her thighs?  A full post about this here.
