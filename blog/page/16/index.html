Dark Ascension Pre-Release Winningness

Jan 30th, 2012

the coveted booster packs

This past Saturday I went to my first sealed tournament for the pre-release of Dark Ascension! We went to Greenlake Games first with a couple of friends, where every table had a nice mat and every participant received a nifty Companion Cube-looking Irondie.

Sealed competitions go like this:
  1. Get 6 packs of cards (in this case, 3 of Dark Ascension, 3 of Innistrad)
  2. Open everything
  3. Make a 40-card deck
  4. FIGHT
First pack rare: Geist of Saint Traft. AHHHH. And in the same pack: Instigator Gang!

I was also excited to try and get some Lambholt Elders and ended up just getting three of them in all of my Dark Ascension packs (and even a holographic one!).

In the end I created a green/white deck splashing both blue and red for Geist and Instigator. I’ve never tried to use four colors before and felt super risky (oftentimes I would take a 5-mana hand just because I had all the colors in it), but when I was not completely mana-screwed I actually did fairly well! I won three of my four sets and won six extra booster packs.

Some memorable moments:
  • In one game: I attacked with Pitchburn Devils, dealt 3 damage to the other player, then next turn brought Pitchburn back with Haunted Fengraf, sacrificed him to equip Somberwald Dryad with Demonmail Hauberk, dealt 3 damage to the other player, then swung with Somberwald who was unblockable due to Forestwalk. The last point of damage was done by casting Fling and sacrificing my 1/2 Lambholt Elder, haha.
  • In my last game: me and the other player were down to dat wire, him with a Galvanic Juggernaut that kept coming back upright with the undying death of his Young Wolf. I took three hits for 15 damage from the Juggernaut but then was at exactly 5 health, which triggered the Fateful Hour of my Thraben Doomsayer and buffed both my Silverpelt Werewolf and Somberwald Dryad. It was close until Andou came by to check out our game and realized that we had both forgotten all about the Fateful hour for the Village Survivors that I had out, since it was covered up by a Chant of the Skifsang – at which point I swung with all my Vigilant creatures and it was over.
  • In another game: my first hand had two plains, three forests, a Thraben Doomsayer, and a Gravetiller Wurm. Over the course of the next couple turns I spawned a human, brought Gravetiller in with Morbid, equipped him with a Demonmail Hauberk and some more Morbid counters thanks to a Hunger of the Howlpack and another human token, and, there it was: a massive 15/13 monster trampling everywhere.

I have Lorin to thank for making full use of Demonmail, since he cleaned me out with it at our last draft and before that point I hadn’t made the connection between Demonmail and how easy it made triggering Morbid since I could sacrifice a creature whenever I wished. Andou read some of the design notes for this set to me; the point of Dark Ascension is that everything is getting dark and awful and worse, the world is teeming with monsters, and I definitely felt the design encouraging me to kill things, to take advantage of death, especially of that of meager 1/1 Human tokens. Fateful Hour was also fun to trigger, though being at the point of death stresses me out, and in general during all the games playing with strangers I could feel my hands shaking with competitive tension.

Later our group went over to The Dreaming to do another sealed tourney, but I didn’t do so well in that one, so, the only memorable moment I’ve got is when Andou pulled a Huntmaster of the Fells and later gave it to me. Dark Ascension werewolves, here I come!

Story in Trauma Team

Jan 24th, 2012

(source)

Time saved from not commuting during snowpacalypse was spent playing Trauma Team, which I ordered (according to this hastily searched-up Amazon receipt) last March.  (Spoilers ahead.)

I adore the time-management micro-activities of Trauma Center, but was still surprised to find out how much I flat-out enjoyed Trauma Team.  The fakey manga cutscenes re-used a lot of character art and implemented some tricks (scaling and translating a car, for example, instead of an actual animation of it driving off into the sunset), but I still enjoyed it much more than any dead-eyed mannequin gestures that seem to fill up so much of the cutscenes I’ve glanced in super-budget games nowadays (though to be completely honest, I haven’t really sat down and played a lot of them).

Cutscenes aside, Trauma Team did a good job of conveying story through its actual gameplay, some memorable scenes being:
  1. The part in the mall where Maria, the “first response” character, has to take care of five dying patients on her own.  I love getting perfect scores on these games and every time someone died I quit and started over in frustration.  I even left the mini-game to try another story-line, and returned later with a feeling of dread – only to realize that it was rigged.  The patients were supposed to be near-death; once you rushed through at least one of them, back-up would arrive and restore everyone to full health, bringing a once-impossible situation to now a somewhat calming one (complete with soothing, don’t worry, your friends are here music).  For that moment, I really was Maria, and experiencing her prime character flaw of over-independence, of trying to do everything myself.  (Until struck the inevitable Japanese group-focused armature of “Let’s all work together!”)
  2. The part where Gabe is diagnosing the soldier, who is struggling to be-“bro” you in an attempt to make it seem you have having ”meaningless, friendly conversation” when in reality you are in the process of diagnosing his viral hemorrhagic fever.  In this case, the characters speaking to each other and working together were embedded in the “verbal questioning” mini-game, which was pretty elegant – a lot more elegant than just clicking through conversation, though the mini-game itself probably lent itself to storytelling pretty easily.
Sometimes you could just see the inconsistencies between Japanese thought and expectation of the US, and how it “really is.” An easy example is when the characters were at the mall – shopping for groceries, which is fairly typical in malls in Asia, and not so much here.

Another example: unlike many US-developed games, this game set in America had a refreshing amount of ethnic diversity in the main character cast.  Can you name a character who was a Mexican woman off the top of your head?  One for whom it didn’t really matter that she had dark skin, one who wasn’t “stereotypically Mexican?”  How about a character who is potentially Native American? The Mexican character was, unfortunately, the subject of many a boob-shot, and was also pretty frustratingly tsundere at times; and in general the females all had the same, petite facial structure.  Personality-wise, though, they were all strong and interesting, with motives that 100% did not involve getting with the men around them.  (Though there was a bit of Maria/That Dark Brooding guy near the end…before he went back to his lifestyle of alternating between doing surgeries and jail-time.)

Some bonus fangirling: the best part of this game, hands-down, were Kimishima’s cases.  I loved piecing together the facts, figuring out how someone had died (though most of them were pretty straight-forward), learning about Kimishima’s weird “I can hear the dead” cellphone and the fact that other characters accepted that she had such a device without complaint (“You and your voodoo cellphone…”).  Gabe Cunningham’s voice actor (apparently the same as for Roy in Full Metal Alchemist) had so much life (the best part being when he sang “chunky~~” at the American politician).  Also, Atlus’s bold, colorful interfaces are so fun to interact with.

[PSCS] Game Design Class 3: Rewards, Goals, and Motivation

Jan 23rd, 2012

This quarter I am facilitating a game design class at Puget Sound Community School for fun and lols.  This is my story.

Other weeks can be found here.

First thing last Tuesday was going over last week’s assigned game, Pixel 2, which has some marvelously colorful particle effects.  Since we had talked a bit about tutorials in the past class, I had asked everybody to keep an eye on how Pixel 2 teaches you how to play the game, and it turns out it does so pretty bluntly.

pixel 2 tutorial screenshot

Everyone agreed that the game was “pretty fun,” mostly because it was very satisfying to collect the pixels, and people enjoyed the way that pixels were shot out at enemies anytime you collected pixels.  We also discovered the “teleportation” aspect of the game, but were mixed about whether it added to the game or not.  The player is moved around with the mouse, and can be held stationary with the space bar while the mouse moves the gun around, shooting at surrounding enemies; but if you release the space bar, the player will automatically “teleport” to the mouse’s present position.

I didn’t get that far in the game due to lack of time and the fact that all the particles make my laptop run slowly, but the students also pointed out that some of the upgrades obtained in the game later are unlabeled, so their function is hard to remember.

After that, we moved on to the topic for the day, which was reward systems.  I referenced a lot of this overview on reward types, though the students were able to name off some rewards easily: leveling up, getting money, getting new items, getting to explore new places.  I talked about schedules for rewards, as well as what I felt was a pretty interesting point from that article:

If you have ever wondered why games with poor game mechanics can still entertain players, it is perhaps because many poorly designed games are at least easy to play - and if they provide rewards according to a reliable schedule, they will entertain…it is the delivery of rewards, and not the quality of the game mechanics, which maintains a player’s interest.

Case in point:  FarmVille, and other games nowadays like EA’s Theme Park which rely on people optimizing for rewards (described with a fair amount of pessimism in who killed video games?).

The next thing I talked about was how the rewards mentioned above were all extrinsic motivations, contrasted with intrinsic motivations, detailed wonderfully in this TEDTalk about “the surprising science of motivation”:




As soon as I described the candle problem one of the students yelled, “What?!  But that’s easy!” right off the bat, so I guess it just goes to show you how they’ve already been brought up with fancy intrinsic motivations (or maybe it just proves that I shouldn’t have drawn the box first, and then the tacks inside the box).

Last topic was goals, the discussion of which I also referenced Only a Game as well as relevant HCI/UX points like Show your player how far they’ve gotten!  Show them all the things they’ve done!  Short-term goals and long-term goals and transparent progress, yeah!

With most topics I usually stop to let everyone try and see what video games may exhibit characteristics of whatever I’m talking about, which leads inevitably to fun tangential discussion about WoW, about achievements, about gamification and whether it works or not.  The only unfortunate thing is that I haven’t played most of the games that they have, since the only console I own that came out in the past seven years is the Wii.  Even playing flash games stresses out the machines I own.

Another thing I learned personally is this: even if the class is going to be mostly discussion and mostly me talking, even if there’s no formal note-taking or tests, it’s good to be writing stuff on the board.  The moment I markered out “extrinsic” people looked up from their laptop screens.  Hurray!

Goodreads Ratings

Jan 23rd, 2012


I’ve been a member of it since 2008, but it wasn’t until recently that I found any affection for Goodreads, a social networking website based on readers and writers.  I haven’t been able to find many people to interact with on it; for me, the greatest value is simply in being able to keep a list of books that I’ve read, and how much I like them, based on a simple scale of 1 to 5 stars.

A scale of 1 to 5 is pretty limited, but can still mean different things to everyone, which is why I like that Goodreads has a little dialog hover helpfully over the stars when you’re judging them.  1 star, it says, stands for I didn’t like it.  2, for It was OK.  3: I liked it.  4: I really liked it.  And 5: It was amazing.

My personal rating system goes like this:

  • 1 – I didn’t like it.  This book made me ill.  Reading it may have caused my opinion of humanity to degrade.  I would grimace when remembering it, and anti-recommend it, fiercely.  Examples: Wild Animus, Bloodborn, Hot Gimmick S.
  • 2 – It was okay.  I wouldn’t read it again or recommend it, but there was some slight redeeming quality about it, even if it was just that it featured a fetish motif (ex. shapeshifters).  Examples: Other, Abandoned, Dead Until Dark.
  • 3 – I liked it.  Reserved for books which kept me turning pages, but in the end were really just so-so.  Examples: Pants on Fire, A Lion Among Men, The Eye of the World.
  • 4 – I really liked it.  Given to books which kept me hungry for more, and were well-written, and had me sitting back dazed for a little after finishing. Examples: Delirium, Wintergirls, The Alchemist.
  • 5 – It was amazing.  My label for books that changed my life, whether in terms of writing style, basic habits, or perceptions of morality/reality/x-ality.  Examples being: The Time-Traveler’s Wife, Perdido Street Station, The Omnivore’s Dilemma.

I read a quote somewhere I can’t find now; the point of it was that it is unproductive to read a book and then wonder to yourself whether you liked it or not.  My takeaway from the idea was that judgment is less productive than analysis – that is, it’s infinitely better to say, “I liked X for Y reasons,” both for yourself and for the writer as well, since the writer is given the opportunity to improve.  But as Goodreads is a social network not just for readers but for writers (with little qualifying labels next to their names), I hesitate at leaving qualitative feedback.  Writers are real!  And they have feelings!  I don’t want to hurt those.  Plus, popular books often have a multitude of reviews praising this or decrying that; I can’t see myself adding anything of value to the discussions, especially when ratings in these cases have usually fallen into an equilibrium rating of 4 or so.

I’ll finish with a quote from A Room of One’s Own by Virginia Woolf:
At any rate, where books are concerned, it is notoriously difficult to fix labels of merit in such a way that they do not come off. Are not reviews of current literature a perpetual illustration of the difficulty of judgment? “This great book,” “this worthless book,” the same book is called by both names. Praise and blame alike mean nothing. No, delightful as the pastime of measuring may be, it is the most futile of all occupations, and to submit to the decrees of the measurers the most servile of attitudes. So long as you write what you wish to write, that is all that matters; and whether it matters for ages or only for hours, nobody can say.

[PSCS] Game Design Class 2: Types of Players

Jan 14th, 2012

Last Tuesday was day two of the game design class I’m facilitating at PSCS. Having no experience teaching game design, it’s mostly an experiment, an exercise in semi-public speaking, and a way for me to talk more about game design, which I really like.

screenshot of five/5

Last week I had the students watch Wolfire’s design critique of World of Goo, and then assigned everybody (including myself) to play ()Five/5, a game we chose at random from the front page of Newgrounds. We were all going to return and talk about it, and these were the major points we came up with about the game:

  • the graphics and sound are nice
  • the mechanic was fun to figure out and work with
  • except the part where the background patterns (varied, moving, and eye-catching) mislead you in thinking they’re relevant, which they aren’t, in that some of us were able to finish without giving them a second thought
  • and, there was also a pretty high uncertainty about whether the game was fundamentally buggy.

Apparently, even after thinking they’re uncovered the secret of the continuity mechanism, there were still times in which two pieces of perfectly-fitting-together map did not allow free passage between them.  This suggests the game may have fared better with a little more feedback; that is, if it had somehow told you when you were doing things wrong (maybe by flashing that nice background), maybe you would be able to differentiate between when you were doing something wrong, or when you were doing something right and the game was messing up (unless the game was really buggy).

The main idea I wanted to discuss this time was that you need to design for your players, so I also had them take the Brainhex questionnaire for funsies to see what type of gamer they were.  Apparently this class is chock full of Mastermind/Conqueror/Achiever types, but someone pointed out quickly that they felt more like a Seeker, and we made a disassociation between identities and behaviors (i.e. it’s more important to design for an “Achiever behavior” rather than “an Achiever person,” since everyone may want different things at different times).  We talked about AI difficulty and what types of behaviors it might please or not.  For example, an AI in a racing game which slows down or speeds up depending on how well or poorly a player is doing would help a novice player because they wouldn’t feel so strongly the sting of defeat.  Someone with Conqueror behavior, however, who loves epic wins, would find an AI that sticks close to her back bumper frustrating – she would only ever win by a hair.  (I got this example from a website whose link I’ve misplaced, but I’ll credit it soon.)

I also talked about PhotoCity (of course!) and the unexpected behaviors that may occur from players.  Our game meant for casual crowdsourcing, became instead a tool for the hardcore player way into 3d building reconstruction.  The “playtest” took the game to a place way different than we expected!  Whoa!  See all the amazing things that can happen after playtesting?

At some point I ran out of things to talk about and had some minutes left so I talked about tutorials, heavily referencing this cool article.  I first asked what the students knew about tutorials and they explained they didn’t have much need of them, since they already knew how to play games for the most part; when they got to a new one, they just clicked through.

Me: “Well, there had to be a time when you didn’t know how to play games already.  What was the first game you played?”

One of them: “I think…yeah, it was Grand Theft Auto 3.”

Oh…oh.

“Haven’t you guys ever played Mario?!”

“A little,” they admitted.  “I haven’t beaten it.”

I drew the first level of Mario on the board anyway and talked a little, and before I knew it, class was up.

I still feel weird about this – I feel simultaneously like there’s not enough to talk about, or not enough interesting things to talk about which don’t involve flashing lights and nice graphics, which is untrue.  At the same time, rather than talk about articles and writing, I wish that I could teach them about paper prototyping, or really try and design a game out that they could program.  But I guess an hour a week for nine weeks has its limitations, and it’s good to start somewhere.

Arkham Horror

Jan 14th, 2012

Last Saturday I played Arkham Horror for the first time.

i HAD to be the author

I’ve heard that it’s a good game so it won’t be new to say that I enjoyed it.  I don’t usually play board games, though when I think back on my elementary school days I remember that Sorry! and Mouse Trap and Uno (which kinda counts, right?) took up the majority of my free time in Latchkey during the pre-Gameboy era.  The most surprising thing about board games now, I have to say, is that the pieces are made of such sturdy material – layers of cardboard with this very pleasant hard matte/muslin texture.  Then again, maybe it’s because this is a Grown Up’s game.

Things I liked about Arkham Horror:

  • Flavor!  The names of places, what you could do in those places, the characters and their abilities, the subtle things written on the cards which explained just enough to chill your mind without sounding cheesy.  You suddenly realize the purpose of the sharp instrument.  Shuddering, you place it back.  Lose one Sanity Point and gain one Clue Token.
  • Cooperation – time has weakened my tolerance for competitive board games.  Now I just love working together to achieve common goals.
  • Being able to change your character’s stats every upkeep – because it’s nice to not be totally locked into a build.
  • The possibility of (fun) epic failure – something that I think is way easier to accomplish in video games than in a board game.  In video games, things can explode with flash and fluff when you miscalculate; in Arkham Horror, you’ve got the Ancient Ones and your imagination.  And the fact that whatever Ancient One that awoke is now screwing everybody over immensely with negative buffs and Endless enemies.

In general, the game had just the right amount of complexity – enough to keep you on your toes, not so much that you were glancing at the rulebook every time you wanted to do something (though we did reach for the rulebook a non-negligible number of times).

HCDE 501 Notes: HCI’s Search for Foundational Theory

Jan 9th, 2012

The field of Human-Computer Interaction started in the 1980s, borne of cognitive and computer science. It started with the idea that humans and computers both have behaviors that are algorithmic, and at a human’s heart is a little processor chipping away at data and outputting actions accordingly. Around this time, Xerox came out with The Graphical User Interface.  It was a novel framework for user interaction, and people were like, “Whoa!” And thus was cognitive science applied to improving GUIs by the millions.

Soon, however, people became dissatisfied. HCI as it was, hand-in-hand with cognitive sciences, was as limited as a college girl off at Ivy League and still dating someone in senior year of high school. They had a lot of good moments the past year, they had some times that were defining of each other’s identity, but in the end, HCI just needed to broaden out, because humans don’t have processor hearts. Suchman (in Plans and Situated Actions) was arguing with AI researchers in the dorm hallway and HCI, having left her door open for potential visitors, overheard.

“Human action is ‘situated,’” Suchman said. Humans respond to opportunity when it knocks, and are flexible to a change in resources. “Computer programs may follow algorithms, but people do not.”

“Just look,” Suchman said, “at all these people who struggled with the design of this photocopier (1987). If people were truly algorithmic, they would have tried to solve their usability problems in the same way.” Alas, everyone found their way to hatred of this copy-machine in a multitude of ways.

This idea, this situatedness, drew right from Ethnomethodology, the guy in the opposite hallway, who grew up with nice sociology parents but had somehow turned out to be unusually rebellious and anti-theoretical. He was all about moment-by-moment actions analyzed as reponses to events in a “local scene of action.” He was all about figuring out what made you (or groups of you) tick, and making you efficient, consistent, coherent. He was best friends with Anthropology, whose whole life was about having comprehensive knowledge about every acquaintance and passerby.

Ethnomethodology was pretty cool, but his parents never got over it. They couldn’t compromise. They tried to keep other people away from him, and to this day Ethnomethodology remained relegated to “a radical antitheory position.” His wish to work above generalization and abstraction were shoved into a corner, and eventually even Anthropology, once a wide-eyed fact-hoarder, left and became theoretical herself.

It was alright. Ethnomethodology continued. He focused. He loved conversation, eventually produced rules about it: “turn-taking,” “repair,” “back-channeling.” In doing so he embraced something his parents had always wanted: generalization. Soon he was spouting things like “highlighting for perception” and “professional vision,” and every multi-syllable ambiguity sent him an inch further down “the slippery slope of theorizing.”

But it wasn’t enough for the kind of foundational theory HCI was really looking for. HCI hung out with him a couple times, but as the years went on their interactions degenerated from brief hellos, strained smalltalk, unanswered Facebook Event invites. Ethnomethodology had an “attitude of indifference” which didn’t seem that stable. HCI kept a memory of empirical studies, of software usage data, but eventually all those nights she spent with him underneath the moon, talking – just, talking, about anything – faded.

HCI needed that foundational theory.  She wanted to learn more, not just about herself and her many facets (Psychology, Sociology, Computer Science, and so on), but about what she wanted to do with her life. It wasn’t so much about trying to make all these different parts of her speak on the same level, but more about having an understanding of her own identity above them all. Without theory she couldn’t compare the past to the present, couldn’t learn about whether she was really becoming better. She couldn’t know whether her next step forward was actually a step back. She couldn’t know whether some pet peeve of hers was typical, couldn’t decide what was important.

Without theory, she couldn’t be as good as Science, who was not useful because she was always real or always right, but because she was always helpful and wanted. Science could stumble, could even make mistakes: but even those missteps led to progress.



Source:
Kaptelini & Nardi, Acting with Technology: Activity Theory and Interaction Design. 2009.
