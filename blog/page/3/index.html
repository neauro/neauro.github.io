Moving to Octopress From Blogger

Dec 15th, 2013

After eight years using Blogger, I’ve finally decided to move away. One of the first reasons I first started using it in high school was because Blogger was one of the only free hosting services I knew where I could also control the styling; but after several Blogger updates, and several years of telling myself I’d eventually learn Blogger syntax well enough to style as I like, I’ve given up. I have other things I’d rather do than invest time parsing Blogger, and Octopres offers more built-in tools and flexibility: I can have nice code snippets and have whatever extra scripts I’d like without worrying they’d interfere with Blogger esoterica. Additionally, after reading this article I’ve been a little more wary of data ownership, and with Octopress, I can host on Github where I know everything is mine.

I’ve also been eyeing ghost recently and though it looks awesome, I decided against it for now for several reasons. Currently, there’s no easy way to import posts from Blogger, and I didn’t want to deal with the extra overhead of figuring out where to host. A couple days ago I received access to Ghost’s subscription hosting, but I don’t want to commit to paying money when this blog is mostly for lolzy purposes.

So, this is how I got my blog set up again:

Get Octopress Up and Running

To actually figure out how to get Octopress running, I referenced several tutorials:

  1. Octopress: Blogging Basics
  2. Webdesign Tuts+: Getting started with Octopress
  3. iAlja’s Blog: Goodbye Blogger, Hello Octopress!

Convert Old Blogger Posts to Be Octopress-Readable

From one of the previous links, I learned how to convert all my old posts to a form Octopress could work with. To do it, open up your old blog in blogger, then go to Settings > Other > Export to download your blog as an XML file. Then, I used this script to run the conversion.

I ran into some issues here, mostly with titles that contained quotation marks or tags that contained colons; whenever this happened I went through the XML and just removed the symbols that were causing things to explode.

Deploy to Github Pages

Finally, I followed these instructions to deploy my blog for free to Github Pages. I still ran into some problems here — when I deployed, the online version of the blog on Github lacked all styles and scripts — this requried a change to the root in config.yml to be /liwanag, since on Github the blog is acccessed through the URL http://neauro.github.io/liwanag. Making this change to root upsets the styles and scripts linking locally, though…so I’ve still got to iron this out and find the magical tweaks that will allow one root value to work both locally and online. There’s also the matter that my blog is big enough that running “rake generate” takes a minute or so to run…but I’m willing to accept that for now.

That’s it!

As one final change, I’ve changed the name of this blog. Previously, it was “nuubu” — a romanization of “noob” spelled in Japanese, chosen offhandedly because I originally intended this blog to be my corner for new things I’ve learned. Because “nuubu” may be appropriative, and because I don’t feel particularly strongly about clinging to the identity of “noob” anyway, I’ve now named it “liwanag,” which means “light” in Tagalog. To be honest, I couldn’t really think of another name at the time, and “liwanag” just sounds nice and makes me think of how blog-writing (that is, writing for an unknwon audience, or “bringing things to light”) helps me think more clearly.

nuubu remains active, because many of the images linked to here point to where they’ve been uploaded on Blogger, and because I don’t want to lose what little bit of popularity those posts have accumulated (specifically, the Rosetta Stone error, and Alpacasso). But overall I’m excited and happy for this bright new world. ✧ʕ̢̣̣̣̣̩̩̩̩·͡˔·ོɁ̡̣̣̣̣̩̩̩̩✧

Allegiant, Veronica Roth

Nov 19th, 2013

Well! It’s over.

I download Allegiant from the Seattle Public Library’s eBook collection as soon as it became available, and read it over the course of a couple days, despite the looming presence of NaNoWriMo (which I’ve been keeping track of in my Tumblr).

[spoilers ahead]

To be honest, I don’t have much to say about it. The writing wasn’t that interesting. There are a smattering of issues that other reviews have already discussed — such as how Tris and Four’s voices were barely distinguishable from each other, something especially tough to deal with when reading on a Kindle. In some places Allegiant felt repetitive. Tris was repeatedly described as either “small” or “pale,” and it felt like eyes carried many of the discussions in this book. (Though I admit I’m not overly fond in the first place of eyes described as communicating incredibly detailed emotions.)

Now she looks pale and small, but her eyes make me think of wide-open skies that I have never actually seen, only dreamed of.
There is a wavering expression in her eyes, like she is a heap of leaves about to be scattered by the wind.
He has kept his eyes averted since I sat in the chair and they cut the strip of plastic binding my wrists together. For just for a moment, his eyes touch mine, and I know it’s time to start lying.
Her fingers are on his throat, taking his pulse, and her eyes are wide and empty. […] Her dark eyes are wide with fear as she says, “Are you hurt?”
She looks up at me, her eyes clear and eager.
She smiles, but her eyes are glassy, like a dormant part of her is fighting its way out and spilling over.

I wasn’t too interested in the big reveal of genetic tampering. Because it was just barely related to any events or concepts introduced in earlier books, it felt totally out of the blue. I could force a connection if I tried hard enough — maybe all the genetic stuff plays into the significance in earlier books about how “one choice can define you” — but ultimately the genetic experiments felt unpleasantly disconnected and abrupt. Reveals are exciting and fun when you anticipate them, when you look back and realize that all the signs pointed toward them; not when they just slap you in the face under the guise of being totally mind-blowing.

I was also disappointed that two of the more obvious characters of color — Tori Wu, and Uriah — were killed off. Uriah’s death acted solely as a catalyst for more of Four’s angst, which he had enough of already, between worrying about his parents and worrying about his genetic purity. Tori’s death accomplished nothing but the sorrow of a secondary character I hardly knew. Their deaths felt like a lazy way to get a rise out of the reader by taking away characters that had always been “good people.”

This is the way that I feel about Tris’s death as well. Her final reason for sacrificing herself wasn’t related to anything else that she desired so desperately in the book: for her community to not die, for her to be with Four. I do like the twist in her character — how she first chose Dauntless, and then, in the end, chose again to be selfless Abegnation — but because I didn’t see how it fit into an overall theme for the third book, this too felt like a cheap way to get me to have emotions about what was going on.

I’ve tried a couple times now to explain more about why the ending of Allegiant was so disappointing to me, but I’ve found this blog post that has already done all of the (super comprehensive) work.

The fact that this ending has, for many readers, “ruined the entire series” also reminds me of this TED Talk, in which the speaker talks about how the end of an experience can ruin the entire thing.

But now there is another question: “How much did these patients think they suffered?” And here is a surprise. The surprise is that Patient A had a much worse memory of the colonoscopy than Patient B. The stories of the colonoscopies were different, and because a very critical part of the story is how it ends. And neither of these stories is very inspiring or great – but one of them is this distinct … (Laughter) but one of them is distinctly worse than the other. And the one that is worse is the one where pain was at its peak at the very end; it’s a bad story. How do we know that? Because we asked these people after their colonoscopy, and much later, too, “How bad was the whole thing, in total?” And it was much worse for A than for B, in memory.

Cultural Differences Across Pokemon X & Y Websites

Nov 4th, 2013

screenshots of the websites for Pokemon X & Y
Japan (above), Netherlands (bottom)

A couple weeks ago we analyzed the marketing sites for Pokemon X and Y across different countries. It turns out that all sites for Pokemon X and Y are the same — except for the one in Japan, which is shown at the top of the screenshot above.

The differences between the Japanese site and the rest-of-the-world sites include:


Feature Descriptions

Non-Japanese sites contain more information about the game, for example, direct links to features like the Pokemon Global Link; on the Japanese site, these are internal.

Hype

Non-Japanese sites feature a large image carousel on their homepage, which shows other features of the game, all shown to excite: Discover the Might of Mega-Evolved Pokemon! A New World of Amazing Pokemon!

In contrast, the front page of the Japanese site merely says: Pokemon, heading to a brand new dimension! (Referring, I think, to the 3D?)

Obvious Marketing

Non-Japanese sites contain immediate links to buy the game, as well as the 3DS; the Japanese site does not. (According to Julius, the 2DS isn’t even available in Japan, perhaps because “Everyone there already has a 3DS!”)


JavaScript

The Japanese site has very little Javascript, and each page looks to be straight HTML. Additionally, text is often baked straight into the images, rather than being left as straight text, which is often the case in websites due to accessibility issues. (Text on images cannot be easily parsed by screenreaders. Some users also prefer to render text on websites to be more readable, for example, through the excellent BeeLine app.

Description vs. Instruction

The Japanese site is more descriptive of the story and characters of the game; the non-Japanese site is more instructive of how to play the Pokemon game.


We chalked up the majority of the differences the fact that Pokemon is more popular in Japan; Japanese game-players may not need to be particularly taught about Pokemon, or persuaded to buy it. But, this exercise makes me more interested in other facets of Japanese web design. Why is there so much baked-in text, why aren’t there fancy hover menu states, why is the design so flat compared to the chrome and bevels of the non-Japanese sites? Is web accessibility less important in the Japanese corner of the internet, or is it handled in a way that’s different in the US?

One final note: out class lecture that week was done by Kate Edwards, current Executive Director of IGDA, who shared some cool stories regarding the “culturization” problems she’s encountered when helping to ship games across international borders. Something she noted was that, in the Netherlands, “adult” games don’t need to be localized into Dutch because the adults there prefer to play the games in English. So, the fact that the Pokemon X and Y site text is all written in Dutch…


…well…you can totally still enjoy the world of Kalos as a grown-up grad student with a full-time job. Just saying!

Gravity

Oct 14th, 2013

(source)

Dr. Ryan Stone is a brilliant medical engineer on her first shuttle mission, with veteran astronaut Matt Kowalsky. But on a seemingly routine spacewalk, disaster strikes. The shuttle is destroyed, leaving Stone and Kowalsky completely alone - tethered to nothing but each other and spiraling out into the blackness. The deafening silence tells them they have lost any link to Earth…and any chance for rescue. As fear turns to panic, every gulp of air eats away at what little oxygen is left. But the only way home may be to go further out into the terrifying expanse of space.

I saw Gravity with A last week. It was alright. People warn that the movie will cultivate in you a brand-new fear of nothingness but I have to say that what terrified me the most was the crescendo of the soundtrack, thickening and filling me with anxiousness before anything had even happened. The death of Goerge Clooney’s Matt also seemed to me to be unreasonably tragic, especially given the fact that he could have been easily rescued.

I would have liked Matt’s sacrifice to be foreshadowed more clearly; the reasons for his final no-nonsense altruism were unclear. Did he sacrifice himself out of a sense of duty? Out of logic? Out of love for Ryan? The story poses life as utterly valuable — worth proceeding with, despite internal struggles and pain — yet allows Matt to give up his existence so easily. I don’t understand how he came to decide Ryan’s life was valuable. I don’t even understand how Ryan herself decided this about her own life at the end, laughing and monologuing and screaming as she fell back to the earth.

I’m biased, maybe — why someone would want to cling to life in the immensity of space is something I was anticipating this movie would explore. Since my personal contemplation of space eventually lends itself to existential panic and dismay that my life is so small compared to a millennia of life and death and nothingness, I wanted to see how this movie would handle it, to see how many mad hyperventilating spirals it would take to get down to hell and come out the other side. I wanted to know what it was people were really missing out there in space, and what was it that kept pulling them back to Earth.

(….eh? Ehhhh?)

Upstream Color

Sep 16th, 2013

(source)
A man and woman are drawn together, entangled in the life cycle of an ageless organism. Identity becomes an illusion as they struggle to assemble the loose fragments of wrecked lives. (via)
[spoilers]

A and I watched Upstream Color this weekend, per the recommendations of various friends and coworkers. As someone on Reddit stated, one great thing about it is that the director does not underestimate the intelligence of his audience. As a viewer, I felt as if I was running to keep up —pausing at one scene with just enough time to grasp at understanding — before rushing on to the next — the next — the next.

A and I noted some scenes with awe at the “show, don’t tell” attention to detail. For example, at some point, the female protagonist says to her love interest, “Let’s buy a car,” and the next scene shows them driving an old vehicle with the price still painted on the windshield. I found the first “hyponosis” scene awesome for its strangeness, and yet so disturbing that I couldn’t bear to watch parts of the protagonist’s violation.

I loved that the medium so closely reflects the characters’ mental state; the choppiness of the timeline mirrors the difficulty that I imagine they must have had trying to reconstruct their lives after having been so compromised. The story also explores some facets (symptoms?) of the hypnotic state that I enjoyed; for example, once the main characters start telling stories to each other, they begin accepting them as their own, and begin arguing about whose memories actually belong to whom.

Upstream Color is great in its focus; it address the exploitation of hypnosis, and the repercussions of it on a human’s brain. (Will the victims ever be okay again? What does “okay” even look like anymore?) Upstream Color requires an intellectual athleticism that leaves you a little sore by the end, in a way that’s not unpleasant.

I Thought My Father Was God: And Other True Tales, NPR National Story Project

Sep 16th, 2013

(source)

The true-life stories in this unique collection provide “a window into the American mind and heart” (The Daily News). One hundred and eighty voices - male and female, young and old, from all walks of life and all over the country - talk intimately to the reader. Combining great humor and pathos this remarkable selection of stories from the thousands submitted to NPR’s Weekend All Things Considered National Story Project gives the reader a glimpse of America’s soul in all its diversity.

I Thought My Father was God is a compilation of short stories submitted by listeners to NPR’s National Story Project, and is fantastic. The events of each story are poignant in their mundanity; the fact that some of these stories were carried throughout the years and then threaded into words gives them a special impact, even if the events themselves are ordinary.

I was the third to be passed this book, after S and A, who read it over the course of several weeks. They found the stories too emotionally heavy to read more than a handful at a time; I found them too delicious to restrain myself. Each story is a good example of how small moments in someone’s life can resonate through years, through actions, through all parts of you; each one reminds me of the impact my own actions can make, however small, and reminds me the the lives of people around me are larger than the glances we exchange as we pass by on the sidewalk.

My favorite story is Iceman of Market Street, especially given recent discussions about the dark side of San Francisco’s transformations.

Siege and Storm, Leigh Bardugo

Sep 5th, 2013

(source)

Darkness never dies.

Hunted across the True Sea, haunted by the lives she took on the Fold, Alina must try to make a life with Mal in an unfamiliar land, all while keeping her identity as the Sun Summoner a secret. But she can’t outrun her past or her destiny for long.

The Darkling has emerged from the Shadow Fold with a terrifying new power and a dangerous plan that will test the very boundaries of the natural world. With the help of a notorious privateer, Alina returns to the country she abandoned, determined to fight the forces gathering against Ravka. But as her power grows, Alina slips deeper into the Darkling’s game of forbidden magic, and farther away from Mal. Somehow, she will have to choose between her country, her power, and the love she always thought would guide her–or risk losing everything to the oncoming storm.

Siege and Storm is the second book in The Grisha Trilogy, and I picked it up because Shadow and Bone was alright, and I like finishing what I start. Siege, unfortunately, felt much messier than Shadow — details and characters were everywhere — they didn’t seem to stick to their identities, and sometimes did things or were capable of things I didn’t understand. Why does Alina, on page 200, suddenly cite an “inner cartographer” when cartography doesn’t affect anything but her description of a single room? How do Mal’s tracking skills lead to him being able to track what should have been a mythical creature, in an environment he’s presumably never been in before? Why is he capable of such observance that he can track animals, but can’t parse Alina’s secrets, despite being her childhood friend? Why does Genya stay with the Darkling in the beginning, instead of fleeing?

Speaking of Genya, when she re-appears later in the story, mutilated, what is it supposed to accomplish? It isn’t to show that the Darkling is evil — we already know that — he’s murdered people and blinded his own mother. Did the Darkling torture Genya for fun? Did he do it because Genya tried to fight him or escape? Did he do it just to bother Alina? The treatment of Genya in particular sickens me, because it accomplishes nothing significant story-wise, and is violent. It reeks of fridging (even though Alina, the protagonist, is female), especially since it was hinted that Genya was sexually assaulted in the first book.

Genya is a flat character; she exists in Siege to be beautiful and then to be “ruined” for no given reason, so harshly that Alina finally develops into someone ruthless. Alina snaps not when she learns thousands have lost their lives to the Darkling and his actions, and not when she learns Ravka’s monarchy is incompetent at best and cruel at worst — she snaps when she learns that there are peasants who call Genya “The Ruined.” Alina thereafter orders repeat offenders to have their tongues cut out.

Maybe the reason why Genya is Alina’s breaking point is because the Darkling’s actions are finally personal to Alina now, but even so, I don’t feel that I have enough of a grasp on Alina’s motivations and feelings to understand how Genya in particular affected her. Throughout the book Alina cites her desire to save her country, to prevent unnecessary deaths; but the deaths of anonymous villagers and even of almost all of the Grisha don’t move her the same way Genya’s mutilation does. Alina’s overall motivations through the book feel hand-waved. Why does she want to save Ravka? Just Because? What has it ever done for her?

The world-building felt especially messy. Both the sea whip and the firebird are creatures that are supposedly famous and well-told in Ravkan stories, but we never hear these stories being told. It’s tough to feel that either are so notorious or venerable without evidence, though I would have really enjoyed reading about them if they had been alluded to more in the first book. I’m also still not clear on what exactly “sun summoning” is, and since there’s a lot made of how Ravka’s canon magic “only messes with what exists, and doesn’t create anything new from nothing” I’m not sure how Alina can just summon light out of nowhere in the first place.

I’m also not a fan of the Russian (Russian-esque?) words thrown into the book at various places. I’m not sure what they’re supposed to accomplish. A sense of exoticism? Why are some words italicized and some not, like “Etherealki” versus ”grenatki,” or “samovar” versus ”kvas”? The flavor is too inconsistent — and for me, too meaningless — to enjoy.

Though I still didn’t find many reasons to like Mal, I really enjoyed reading about Siege’s newest character, Nikolai, to extent that I wished that the entire book was about him instead, haha. Nikolai, a rebellious privateer prince who desperately cares for others and deeply understands human nature, is way more compelling than another naturally handsome teenage peasant whose biggest problem is his commoner’s bloodline. Bardugo’s dialogue in general also has Whedon-esque punchyquipness and I loved reading it.

For the third book: I’m excited to see what reasons the Darkling had to commit all the atrocities he did. I really am! I also hoped Nikolai survived. Please. Think of the quips!

Lone Survivor & Meaningfulness

Sep 5th, 2013



0.

Lone Survivor was the game I was most excited to obtain through a Humble Indie Bundle months ago, because the big grainy pixels looked so delicious, so fat with mood and atmosphere. And weirdness! And mysteriousness!

Spoilers ahead.


1.

One of my favorite parts of adventure games is the characterization that rises through interaction with items. Do characters like a certain item, or dislike it? Does the item bring up deep heartbroken memories of the past? How do they parse and react, and what does their rumination say for who they are? I’m reminded of Nancy Drew’s endearing enthusiasm for lifting up random objects (“This plastic DNA model will be useful!”) and Phoenix Wright’s skeptical amusement when things like puppies are added to his inventory.

The most interesting item in the game for me was the toy cat, which I enjoyed petting all the time to accrue invisible “sanity” points. Engaging with the cat toy in particular puts you in the shoes and brain of the lone survivor himself, tinkering with a plush at the end of the world, just to have something light-hearted to do.



2.

Speaking of the cat plush, I loved the detail of this game — the unidentifiable flesh-like substances stretching across the wall — the way your map was dark unless you viewed it under sources of light — the specific food items like Delicious Ham, the creepy bottomless repositories of pills and Rotten Meats.



3.

A criticism of Lone Survivor seems to be that it was not scary enough. I played it at the height of summer and even then it was plenty terrifying, even if most of my stress and fear stemmed from being unable to hoard my bullets as obsessively as I would have liked.

Another scary part: putting your hand in that horrifying pink-red flesh-pixel hole. I really did not want to do that.


4.

The dream sequences were cool, and the characters therein intriguingly surreal, which I loved. Ultimately, however, I didn’t feel that these scenes contributed to a larger understanding of what was going on, which was disappointing. Rather than deep, the first ending I received was vague, and a letdown. After carrying and caring for this dude I’d wanted a little more than a whiff of a broken heart. I wanted to know more about what this girl meant to the protagonist, how her departure left his life with zombie-filled corridors.


5.

I got my ending by playing as paragon as possible. I wanted to see if other endings would give me closure, so I found them on Youtube. Each one seemed vaguer than the last, and didn’t interest me that much. I don’t have a particular love of multiple endings and for me it’s because they lack impact: I’ve only rarely encountered any set that comfortably resolves all issues and tension introduced in the game, and the way that such endings are reached never quite seems to match the decisions you made to get there.

In the case of Lone Survivor, the decisions that you make add to an invisible calculator which eventually spits out one of these endings: for example, killing more monsters gives you endings that imply you’re mentally unstable. But why? What is it about killing these things makes you this way? The worst ending can also be achieved not just by killing monsters, but “eating food when not hungry or hurt” and “sleeping for no reason” (per this wiki). What’s that connection?


6.

This was my other favorite part: getting that white cat to come live with me. I guess it’s strange to be saying that after my demands for meaningfulness, since other than the invisible points, hanging out with the cat doesn’t reveal any more of the protagonist’s inner story. But playing with the cat, I felt for the first time that I was doing something in the game that I personally wanted, like it was really me in there, and not me poking through things in a dude-shaped glove.

Come here! Come here, little digital thing!

come and be with me forever

PAX 2013 Thoughts, & Cosplay: Red From Transistor

Sep 4th, 2013

I loved Bastion and I’m super super super excited for Transistor, because it has a female protagonist with curly hair. Curly hair!

(Or at the very least, wavy hair!)

I was not keen on attending PAX this year until I found a friend selling tickets and my compulsion to hoard scarce resources broke through my skepticism of the event. I first went in 2009 and stopped out of boredom and discomfort, but decided I’d give this year another try, not because I think trivializing rape is okay (I don’t) but because I wanted to see if the environment has changed since Tropes Vs Women. I also really, really, really wanted to see Transistor.

Since I got my tickets late, I started cosplay work only about a month before PAX started, which also happened to be right in the swing of summer quarter. Making the sword was fun and challenging, mostly because it was my first time working with plastic. First I made some sketches and then a model using some leftover foam from my Strength cosplay.

such cosplay wow

Then I visited TAP Plastics and after beating around the bush I admitted that I was making a giant sword. Turns out the guy staffing was also a cosplayer and happy to give me advice, which I desperately needed. I had my heart set on a blue-y transparent plastic, which they didn’t have, but he explained that if I painted on the backside of clear plastic, it would look like a mirror…and that sounded great! I got a couple samples of green-edge acrylic and tried out a couple different paint colors on it.

i was so bright-eyed then

A pointed out that what would really make the sword striking would be a strong contrast between the base color and “solder lines,” so for the base color I decided on using a lot of green to really make the inner lines stand out.

Then I visited TAP Plastics to get the pieces cut, and at the last moment decided I wanted not just half an inch thickness of plastic, but a whole inch. Imagine my shock and horror when I realized that such a width of plastic is actually heavy. Like, really heavy. (I don’t have a scale, but a friend’s estimate when he picked up the final product and grimaced was 35 lbs, so I’m going with that number.)

heavy, but still not as unwieldy as strength arms

My plan was to do a reverse painting deal on both sides of the sword and then sandwich them together. I used masking tape to copy out my drawn pattern on both sides, and first painted in the solder lines with a mixture of paints: pearly blue, shiny white, and glow-in-the-dark!

left photo courtesy of couch

I didn’t get to see the glow paint in action, unfortunately, and the fact that it’s sort of thick and grainy made me nervous that the plastic pieces would stick together properly when pressed together.

peeling the tape off was so satisfying

I had two circular pieces of plastic that I wanted to attach to the outside of the sword for the Transistor’s red “eye,” but there’s also a sort of depression/border around the eye. I realized that in my limited time I wouldn’t be able to do anything three-dimensional for that border, so I decided to just paint another blue circle.

dry dry dry! (also there’s A, because i need another picture to even this out)

Meanwhile, the eye got the same “reverse painting” treatment.

i see you

Once the solder lines were done, I combined green-y metallic paint and blue metallic paint and painted that down across both pieces.


such contrast

Since I was swamped with making the actual costume, A helped out with the handle and cutting out what we referred to as the “top fins” and “side fins” from plywood. Because the plastic turned out to be so heavy, and because we were pressed for time, we left the handle rectangular, rather than shaved down to the canon cylinder.

plywood fins

After that, with his father’s help, A and I clamped the two pieces together in a vise and drilled holes through the wood and plastic! Again not canon, but the pieces were such heavy wow, and needed that extra reinforcement.

so close!

After drilling, we had planned to take the sword apart and touch up the paint a bit, but…after all the clamping, the two pieces of plastic ended up completely stuck together. So much for worrying about it coming apart.

the light does cool shading tricks through the plastic

The last thing to be built was the pommel, which A flashed out overnight. I have no pictures of it, but it was made out of some plastic canisters and a plastic soysauce dish hot-glued together and painted over. Cosplay at its finest. :’)

Finally, what was left was to paint the plywood pieces, and then glue on the red eye. A also put on some electrical tape to make the side fins appear more indented, but the tape near-immediately fell off during peace-bonding. Ah well!

done!

As for the dress itself…I reused the racer-back tank pattern I made for the Strength cosplay, though I made it longer and added a kind of cape for the sleeves. I wanted to sew the jacket as well but ran out of time and ended up compromising by sewing a satin triangle to a long black jacket I found at a consignment store a couple days before PAX. The last thing I did was the feather collar, which I made by sewing feathers into leftover Strength felt and sewing it around the dress’s neck.

feathers!

On the day before PAX I also quicked out the triangular ring from Sculpey (also left over from Strength cosplay; thank goodness I can reuse so much stuff) and acrylic paint.

photo by couch

Sooo overall…though there are a couple things I want to re-do for the next round (including remaking the feather collar and ring), I finished a new cosplay and am proud of my new forays into the world of plastic! PAX itself was fun in and of itself; I was really happy to finally play Transistor, and even more excited for it once I actually played and recognized all the programming nods. I was a little bewildered by the inclusion of the “stop time and attack” thing, until I realized it was so thematic — since the Transistor’s atacks are sort of like methods, “stopping time” to queue up attacks and execute them really mirrors compiling programs. Uwaaa.

I was also surprised and happy to see so many cool Reds at PAX, many of whom had taken the extra leap to use ~*electricity*~! I briefly ran into the Red from Breakers Cosplay who was super impressive and inspiring. I also met a lot of progressive dudes and women who specifically talked about how awesome it is to have A Female Protagonist in a game (something I don’t think I would have never heard anyone say flat-out in years past), and a lot of friendly people in general who gamely attempted holding my Transistor and remarked at how they admired my ridiculousness for carrying it around all day at the convention, haha. To be fair, my arms were really sore afterward, but all my rock climbing callouses helped out quite a bit!

My favorite moment in all of PAX was to meet Jen Zee, who was so awesome and kind and asked for pictures in which I tried desperately to look cool despite my euphoria. She also gave me a ton of Supergiant swag that I placed all around my house immediately. :’)

i christen thee, supergiant wall

There was also a magical instant where A and I, passing curiously by the Paramount, suddenly realized that Darren Korb and Ashley Barrett were performing and dashed inside as quickly as possible and caught the Transistor song!

All this being said, I had a great weekend, mostly due to the friends and people I was able to meet. I don’t think I feel comfortable enough to return again (because reasons), but I am thankful for my personal experience this year.

especially for time runnin around with couch!

And finally, now that both my summer quarter classes and PAX are completely finished, I am free!

…and ready to devote myself to a new project! Maybe something for the Tower Jam?
