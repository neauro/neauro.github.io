MTG: My Second Ever Draft

Dec 19th, 2011



This last Sunday me and some friends (specifically the “Dat House” crew who had all at some point resided in a drafty, strange-smelling pink house near campus) held our own MTG draft.

My first pack: Geist-Honored Monk. Okay. Nice. I started grabbing Chapel Geists and also, at some point, a Cloistered Youth, Screeching Bats for the bomb factor, a Falkenwrath Noble. There’s just something so classy about white/black.

Second pack: Hinterland Harbor, Innistrad’s rare blue/green land, something I’d like but which doesn’t fit the colors I’m primarily using. Shoot. Luckily Andou was the person I was passing to, and I was sure that he would take it. >)

Third pack: Skaab Ruinator.  Which pretty much required that I start using blue along with my other two colors.

Figuring out how much mana is appropriate for a deck is what I think is the hardest about deck-building.  It was a struggle especially in this one, since I had Screeching Bats, Geist-Honored Monks, and the Ruinator – each of which require two black, two white, and two blue mana respectively, which makes selecting colors harder.  Still, this deck did pretty well; at the very least there were few cases in which I didn’t have mana at all, and when I had Skaab Ruinator I always managed to push him out successfully.

Minesweeper Search

Dec 14th, 2011

Since it was free on iTunes some time ago (and then again yesterday), I downloaded Minesweeper of Wizard. I never did learn how to play Minesweeper with any complexity beyond a simple random-peck method until my smiley-face guy suddenly had dead “X” eyes, but it turns out Minesweeper is simple enough. When you click a square and it reveals a number to you, that number is representative of how many bombs are located in the neighboring squares. I have to admit that I am probably more pro at this game than I ever could have been before thanks to a course I took in computer vision, a.k.a. Find The Neighbors of a Pixel and Do Something 101.

Minesweeper of Wizard ties several stages of minesweeping up around a loose story. You are a wizard.  You need to surface the world from darkness.

adorable graphics

Each stage is graded from one to five stars depending on how fast you completed the game. And it was because of this that I stopped playing on level 5.

i hate you

The type of person/player that I am is emotionally uncomfortable with passing onto next levels until I have completed things 100%. I did pretty well on the first four levels, but for some reason I just cannot get more than four stars on that fifth level. I started with a finish time of 1:20, played the level over and over again, managed to whittle my time all the way down to 0:40 – and what do I get? 4 stars.

IMPERFECTION.

Andou pointed out that some people can finish Minesweeper in a matter of eyeblinks, and yes, it’s true that I did somehow manage to improve and cut my time down by more than half. But still, this is level 5. How hard are the other levels going to be?!  And it’s not just your pure logic that factors into your time – the game itself is the first I’ve played for iOS that actually feels non-responsive, and also lags.

Minesweeper thirst having awakened, I went off to find a new app. The one that I liked the best of the free apps I found is Minesweeper Free+ by Cookie Balloon.

Minesweeper Free+
Pros:
  • Chrome/Glowy graphics are nice.
  • No lag!
  • It provides multiple board sizes (easy, medium, hard).
  • It’s exactly what it is.

Cons:
  • The little “flag a square!” button on the top-right is teensy and hard to press, and there’s no visual feedback on the board that shows whether it’s been selected or not. Result: I often try to flag a square where I know a bomb is, and instead just set it off. :(
  • The message for “YOU WIN!” feels a little anti-climatic/un-victorious. I just spent like 7 minutes poking this puzzle! I wish there was a score or some other way that it tallied up how you did…maybe show a Bejeweled Blitz-esque graph or something of your progress second by second…ah well.

[Microaggression] Tumblr Posts Where There’s Some Girl Posing by a Nerd Thing and Other People Mock Her

Nov 25th, 2011


I feel like I run into a lot of posts on Tumblr which have this formula:

  1. Find picture of a girl posing “attractively” near a nerd-related thing; ex. a girl placing her cheek next to a game controller or TCG item and staring into the camera
  2. Reblog, with a photo of yourself contorting your face next to a nerd-related thing

It’s meant to be funny, but I think the underlying humor of this joke is related to these idea that girls shouldn’t be showing themselves off to people. (Sluts!)

Part of the funny may relate to the assumption that this girl is using cards to appear “nerdy,” which is nowadays ”a new stereotype of femininity.” You can’t tell that this is the case from a single picture.  I mean, how could you rationalize it?  This girl doesn’t really like Yu-Gi-Oh! cards!  She’s just showing off!  Yeah!  Go sit back in your corner and where no one can see you, shoo!  Only then can you really be a True Nerd.

From Re-Space-ing Place: “Place” and “Space” Ten Years On:
My goal [in this paper] is to understand two related developments over the last decade. One is the way in which the conceptual distinction between place and space has been taken up by [computer-supported cooperative work] researchers, and to see what kinds of light we might be able to cast on this by returning to the roots of this distinction in fields such as geography and urban studies that have something to say about the social organization of spaces. The other is the ways in which our heightened understanding of the issues of mobility and information as an aspect of everyday life, emerging out of studies and experiences with mobile technologies, might reinvigorate a conceptual exploration of questions of practice and spatiality, and help us deepen our understandings of the implications of arguments about place and space.

I Seriously Notice This All the Time, Which Probably Means It Could Be Made Better

Nov 15th, 2011

Bus drivers in Seattle will sometimes talk to passengers as they enter the bus. As passengers step up, the drivers will put their hands over the little fare box to obstruct it, and repeat, loudly, “Pay as you leave. Pay as you leave. PAY AS YOU LEAVE.”

Alas! It’s too late. The person was talking on their cellphone and already swiped their Orca card on the Orca card reader.

photo by JeanineAnderson (source)

After doing this, the bus driver will usually just sigh.

Orca is (essentially) just a fancy debit card for public transportation in some counties around southern Puget Sound. To use, you fill it up with money and then press it against the Orca card reader, which is located further away than the bus driver can reach to block it from hasty passengers responding to habit and the design of the bus entranceway. Swiping your Orca card incorrectly may result in some weird things happening with your fare (things possibly related to how your transfers are monitored), and though I don’t know the exact repercussions of paying when you enter the bus instead of when you exit, it is kind of sad to see bus drivers being frustrated with this, especially since I learned in an Informatics class last year that bus drivers have some pretty serious rules governing their work.

Also especially since I feel like there are a couple ways you could mitigate this frustration:
  • Move the Orca card reader closer to the bus driver. Then the bus driver could reach over and cover the card reader much in the same way I see them covering the fare box. I think this could be the least expensive solution.
  • If someone tries to pay when they are not supposed to, the Orca reader shouldn’t charge them. There are already technologies in use that make use of what I assume is a capability to know where a bus is at a certain time, which could be used to know when people should be charged, and for what rate. (Presently drivers fiddle with the Orca card readers to make sure that people get charged properly, which seems like it may be an unnecessary task.) People try to pay for the bus all the time in the Ride Free area and it doesn’t matter how many nice, green “Ride Free” signs there are, they just don’t read – so just account for it. Extra advantage: bus driver thinks less about fares in general, and more about just driving. (Disadvantage: This doesn’t account for cash fares. Also, how are you really supposed to know when people are entering or exiting, to figure out whether they should be charged?
    • Use cameras to measure differences in position to determine a passenger’s direction?
    • Count the number of times a door opens and closes?
    • Or, since you don’t really need to touch Orca cards directly to the Orca card reader and James keeps talking about how easy it is to sniff data from Orca cards, maybe the transaction should just happen as people are standing on the bus, so they don’t pause to pay with an Orca at all. Thus you could have something like: a) Charge everyone as they get on the bus, b) If a person leaves the bus in a Ride Free area, put the money back on the card, c) Else, if the person leaves the bus after leaving the Ride Free Area, just leave the charge on the card.)

Granted, these are all probably past the power of our struggling transportation system (though I admit I only describe as struggling due to Prop. 1 failing recently and grumbling anecdotes on Facebook about the inefficiency of buses).
