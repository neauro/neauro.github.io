Books of 2012

Jan 6th, 2013

I finished total of 114 books last year. That significantly beats my running average of 15 books per year since 2008 (though I admit I haven’t been as avid a user of Goodreads before this year). Before anyone starts getting intimidated, this total is comprised of books of a variety of lengths and content, including: young adult fiction; super grown-up adult fiction; delicious non-fiction probably about the history and political atmosphere surrounding a specific food item; graphic novels and comic books; albums of photography; at least half a dozen light novels; and one James and the Giant Peach.

I owe it all to two things:

  1. Less school — who knew that only taking evening classes would leave you with so much free time?
  2. So much commuting — a.k.a. two hours of dedicated reading time every day.

And so, without further ado:


Memorable Books of 2012


Most Life-Changing

Wired for Story, Lisa Cron & Reading Like a Writer, Francine Prose
(source: Goodreads)

Among all of the non-fiction that I read this year, these two books together were personally the most “life-changing” ones, because before I even finished either of them I felt my entire outlook on reading, writing, and story completely changed. Wired on Story focuses on scientific concepts and general “story architecture,” whereas Reading Like a Writer hones in on recognizing and interpreting writing on the “micro” scale, and on holding each word to the light to see its color. Together they were both hugely inspiring and super influential to me, for whom reading and story-thinking takes up a lot of the day.


Most Mind-Boggling Concept

Sword Art Online: Alicization Running, Reki Kawahara
(source: Goodreads)

I read the entire Sword Art Online light novel series as soon as I started getting impatient to see upcoming episodes of the anime. They were all fine and didn’t quite have the visual appeal or charm of the anime, but I’m singling out the most recent volume because of its completely unexpected what the fuck factor, which involves (spoilers) cloning the “human soul” part of babies’ brains, and placing them into a virtual environment to see how they “grow up.” Researchers monitor decades and generations of these isolated, disembodied humans in ease, as every year in the virtual environment lasts only a minute in the real world (or something like that). At some point one of the researchers experiments with cloning his own brain into a computer, and when they turn on the speakers, they hear the researcher’s voice, aghast and desperate: “What do you mean, I’m the clone? I’m the real one! Let me out of here!”



Most Shippable

Kamisama Hajimemashita, Julietta Suzuki
(source)

I started reading this waaay back when it came out and returned to it after its anime series started airing. The anime was surprisingly amusing and charming but the manga is so much better, and Nanami’s increasingly time paradox-y story is definitely my defining fangirl obsession for this year, haha.


Most Tears Shed

The Fault in Our Stars, John Green
(source: Goodreads)
;___;


Most “Shoot, Everything That I Thought I Knew is False”

Lies My Teacher Told Me, James Loewen
(source: Goodreads)

It’s hard to describe this book, because I don’t think any description could do it justice. It’s a book that I think everyone should read if they’ve ever had to put themselves through high school US history courses. It discusses not just the whitewashing of history, but the strange and problematic air of idolization held for history figures like President Woodrow Wilson, and the politics of textbooks with regards to concepts like classism. It also has a ton of interesting stories about historical people of color. (For example, did you know that there was a Native American named Squanto who was captured by Englishmen, then somehow avoided slavery and made his way to London, then tried to make it back to North America and ended up back in England again, and then finally returned to North America some years later? Whoa!)


Most “Wow, That was Really Good, I Really Wish There was More”

(source)
As aforementioned.


Most “Wow, I Can’t Believe I Enjoyed That”

East of Eden, John Steinbeck

It was really only surprising because I have such a tough time enjoying “classic” literature, but I found myself mesmerized by East of Eden and even stole it from A to read desperately while we were waiting to board vacation flights. This book was the first time that I noticed how nuanced and powerful writing could be especially when it is simple and unadorned. To my utter surprise it also featured the coolest Chinese character ever.


Here’s to a brand new year of reading wonderful books :D

Rise of the Guardians: Wow, That Was Legit

Dec 30th, 2012

(source)

After a lot of hemming and hawing because of the fact that it scored “only” 74% on Rotten Tomatoes, I decided to drag A along to see it in theaters because I figured the animation was worth seeing at least once on a huge screen.

I really liked it, much more than Wreck-It Ralph, the other visually ebullient film that I can’t help comparing it to.

Things I Really Liked About RotG (with spoilers for both it and Wreck-It Ralph below):


  • Jack’s problem is clearly defined: I don’t know why I’m here, and I want to be seen. By the end of the film, Jack’s character also undergoes a transformation to solve his problem: My purpose is to share my love of “fun” with children, and now they believe in me. In contrast, Ralph’s “transformation” seemed weak and frustrating to me personally; at the beginning he is a character who longs for recognition and acceptance, and at the end becomes happy because he gets…recognition and acceptance. It’s just a little boring. It’s like a story about someone who says, “You know what I would like? $5.” And then that someone meets a princess who gives him $5. The end!
  • The other primary characters interacted with Jack in ways that helped him with his transformation. Santa teaches Jack the idea of a “center,” or something that Jack is all about, which Jack realizes he doesn’t know. Tooth teaches Jack that he was someone before he was Jack Frost, and later is an example of how the Guardians have become misaligned with their previous love of caring for children. Bunny is a sort of litmus test for Jack’s familiarity with the Guardians, and the Sandman’s death was what triggered Jack’s supercoldblizzard power (or whatever). The antagonist, Pitch, was also a foil for Jack, representing the “bad side” of wanting to be seen and recognized so badly that you would want children to fear you. In contrast, the interactions between Ralph and characters like Felix and Jane Lynch Sergeant Tamora were pretty shallow, and after the Hero’s Duty part of the movie, whenever you saw Tamora it was really just for romantic comedy fluff that had nothing to do with the movie’s title character.
  • The Tooth Fairy had her own not-man-related things going on, including but not limited to control of whatever the fairy army is called and a love of gummy, bloody teeth that was amusingly ridiculous. Compare to the Sergeant, whose motivation is hate for the bug-things because they killed her husband (in her fake memories). It’s true that Tooth was fangirly about Jack’s teeth and all of her fairy minions were swooning over Jack’s hoarfrost bangs but I’ll take what I can get.
  • No romance! (Beyond that too-long hug at the end between Tooth and Jack?) Just, thank you for not stuffing that into the movie, especially since it was already bursting its seams with all the fast-paced kingdom-touring and gold particle effects. (On a related note, #rainbowsnowcone and #frostbite seem to be what the shippers are calling Frost/Tooth.)

There were a couple plot holes in the movie that bothered me somewhat (Why did they only need to round up teeth for one night to save Tooth? After everyone fell asleep in Jamie’s room, why were they suddenly in the sleigh, still-sleeping, later?) but I have been reading Wired for Story by Lisa Cron recently, and one of its tenets is that good stories are not about what just events that happen, but about the changes that happen in their characters. I’m not ready to forgive all plot holes for this reason, but it does give me a new lens into what details are the most important in story. In the case of these two movies, Jack’s change from the invisible, meaningless delinquent into someone that uses his fun for good is what made him so much more compelling to me, and enjoyable to watch as well.

Blind Willow, Sleeping Woman, Haruki Murakami

Dec 15th, 2012

(source)

This is a compilation of Haruki Murakami’s short stories. I’ve never read Murakami’s short stories before, but enjoyed these ones, mostly because reading novels by Murakami for me is like falling into a dark, magical pit, and upon landing at the bottom you are left winded, stranded, and a little bit at loss but convinced that you saw sparkling things on the way down so it was all pretty cool overall.

In contrast, reading each short story here was like eating green tea pudding. Your mouth, meeting the first bite, finds it bitter and creamy and bewildering; meeting the second, acclimates, and thinks, Oh wow, okay, whoa, this is great; and then you’re opening your mouth for the next mouthful and there’s nothing but spoon.

Wait! But – what –?

I have no idea. But those first two bites were pretty good.

New Portfolio Layout, Woo~

Dec 9th, 2012


Hurray!

I have to say it’s a pretty huge improvement over the old layout I’ve had for over a year now, and it’s due to the fact that I can do a ton of front-end work at my new job. I can’t believe how much I’ve learned over the past four months, including but not limited to standard stuff like jQuery that I have always wanted to practice but hadn’t found the time for.

This particular layout makes use of a new CSS trick that I learned, and really like – a way to resize the “top” div of a website so that it always fits in the space above the fold, no matter the size. All you need is a container div, and inside that two more divs: one for the area above the fold (the “landing”), and one for your “main” information.

The CSS looks like:

html,body {
margin:0px auto;
height:100%;
}

div#container {
min-width:100%;
height:100%;
}

div#landing {
width:100%;
height:100%;
background: #5d5d5d url("../images/header_bg.jpg") center center no-repeat;
background-size:cover;
}

div#main {
background-color:#eeeee2;
}

And the HTML should be like:
<body>
 
    top part of page
  
 
    the rest of the page
  
</body>

How exciting.

Coconut/Orange/Pineapple Sorbet

Nov 29th, 2012

sor-bay? sher-bet?

I made my first “real” sorbet today! (“Real” being my way of distinguishing this sorbet from my actual first sorbet-making experience, in which I fastidiously measured out a cup of pineapple juice into a sorbet mixture, and then poured into the ice cream maker not this mixture, but the leftover pineapple juice. Whoops.)

I really adore ice cream and gave an ice cream maker to my mother last Christmas, which she gave back to me after admitting she’d probably never use it. Molly Moon’s book – with its color, environmentally friendly morals, and cheering that you can really do it, it’s easy! – is what really got me interested in trying to make ice cream. But, since it was on hold at the library, I found a different book to use for initial experiments.

this one! (source)

Recipe of the Week: Ice Cream: 52 Easy Recipes for Year-Round Frozen Treats is just as simple as Molly Moon’s book, and it emphasizes using more fresh ingredients to enhance ice cream flavor. This is my source for the sorbet above, which is really just coconut milk, pineapple juice, and orange juice, with a little bit of cinnamon on top because it seemed like it needed something for the photo.

I didn’t realize making ice cream was so easy. The ice cream maker, a contraption I always regarded as impermeably mysterious for some reason, is in fact just a sort of canister with frozen stuff in it, in which you’re supposed to whip up your mixture such that it freezes along the sides and gets filled with air in the process. Most ice cream recipes seem like some variation of “Put cream and other things into a saucepan, bring it to a simmer, let it cool, then throw it in an ice cream maker.”

Here’s to a cool future.

Molasses Cookies

Oct 29th, 2012

Now that I finally have my mixer parts back from Molly (who had taken great care of them for about a year, after I accidentally left them behind in our old place…haha;;), I am enthusiastic for all matter of baking projects.

In particular, these molasses cookies, which are super chewy and wonderful.

that’s a REAL MAPLE LEAF back there! fell the autumn spirit!

The following recipe is from my mother, altered slightly:

(yields 5 dozen)

Ingredients
12 tablespoons butter
1 cup sugar
1 egg
1/4 cup molasses
2 tablespoons evaporated milk or cream
1 teaspoon vanilla extract
2 1/2 cups all-purpose flour
1 1/2 teaspoons baking soda
1 tsp. ground cinnamon
1 tsp. ginger
1/4 tsp. ground coriander
1/4 tsp. ground white pepper
1/4 tsp. cayenne pepper
3/4 tsp. salt
3/4 tsp. ground nutmeg (best to grind your own)

Instructions
In a large bowl, cream butter, shortening and sugar, until fluffy/airy. Beat in egg, molasses, milk and vanilla. Combine flour, baking soda, cinnamon, coriander, white pepper, cayenne pepper, salt, and nutmeg; gradually add to creamed mixture. Cover and refrigerate for 1 hour.

Roll into 1 1/4 inch balls. Place 2 inches apart on greased baking sheets. Bake at 350 degrees for 10-14 minutes or until tops crack and edges are slightly firm. Remove to wire racks to cool.

Butternut Squash Soup

Oct 29th, 2012

Ever since my old roommate Molly made butternut squash a couple years ago, I’ve really wanted to try making it myself. My first experience with it was like whoa. Who knew such decadence could some from such an unassuming squash?

I found a recipe from the online version of Cook’s Illustrated to try out, since other recipes I found toss in tons of cream and recommend roasting the squash to dehydrate it and bring out its flavor, and I didn’t want to go through the extra effort and ingredients. What no one mentions, however, is that the most time-consuming part of cooking butternut squash soup is actually processing the squash in the first place. I tried three different knives to peel and cube this thing, each less sufficient than the last.

@_____@

The next obstacle was my lack of a Dutch oven, which is what the recipe suggests you cook everything in. An internet search seemed to suggest that any old pot with some foil put over the top of it would suffice, so I tried that, but then came across Problem #2: my pot was way too small to fit all the squash and leeks, much less another four cups of vegetable broth. So, I decided to cook two halves of the soon-to-be-soup separately, except probably this pot was really not the best pot to use in the first place, because it became severely burnt.

i really doubt this is the “fond” that’s supposed to form at the pot’s bottom

I moved to a shallower pan and that seemed to work.

…maybe?

By the time all of the squash had been boiled down, it still wasn’t quite…soupy.

:C

I thought I would be okay without a blender, but the soup at this stage was very fibrous and chunky and unappealing. Enter Problem #3: I have no food processor!

Luckily the neighbors do, so we ran over there just to make use of their Vitamix.

YES. IT LOOKS LIKE SOUP. YESSS

Final touch: a swirl of sour cream, which sounded questionable but actually complemented the flavors very well.

woo!

It was delicious! Hopefully I’ll have the chance to make this recipe again before the season’s out.
