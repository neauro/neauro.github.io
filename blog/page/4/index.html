I’ve Been Doing Some Mobile Web Work Recently.

Jul 25th, 2013

I’m unenthusiastic when it comes to designing websites for small screens like phones, probably because I don’t have a smartphone. (No particular reason why — just that my 6-year-old palm-sized brick of a phone is sturdy and unexciting to steal, and I plan on staying with it as long as it stays with me.)

jQuery Mobile is what I’ve been learning recently; it is a “mobile framework” and “HTML5-based user interface system,” which means that you can
  • stick it in your project
  • throw in some common “mobile” UI elements like headers and buttons, or even switches
  • have those buttons be styled to look pretty good already!

Function-wise, you can also use jQuery Mobile to
  • transition very prettily between pages and dialog boxes
  • easily support ARIA attributes for better accessibility across your UI elements

jQuery mobile was confusing for me at first because I would write simple markup, but when viewing it online, my bare elements would suddenly have tons upon tons of classes — a div with a class of mobile-header, for example, would suddenly be a div with the classes mobile-header ui-header ui-bar-a, and would also have a data-role="header" as well. Links in my mobile-header would have also been transformed into elements with tons of classes: icon ui-btn-left ui-btn ui-btn-up-a ui-shadow ui-btn-corner-all…

Which brings me to my next point about jQuery Mobile: it is a pain to style into your theme. Because elements can be so deeply nested, it’s hard to figure out what exactly is making all of my links black when they should be blue, and so on. Trying to make a link blue may require that you overwrite not just one style but two. jQuery provides a theme roller which you can use to change the default black style downloaded in the package, but as the things I want to make are radically different than the rounded, chrome buttons they offer, a theme roller doesn’t help much.

Regarding getting started, I found that this blog post and the ones following were quite helpful. Additionally, this link on Rethinking the mobile web is a great primer on why developing for the mobile web is important (despite all my eye-rolling/teeth-gnashing) and gives data and another perspective on “mobile-first” design processes.

Exploring Animal Crossing, & Not-Aloneness

Jul 17th, 2013

My character, Hito, and Felicity

I haven’t written much because I’ve started summer classes and also spending most of my recreation time in Shiba, which is the name of my Animal Crossing town. For my birthday weekend (some month ago now), I went all-out and scoured my hometown until I found the last remaining Animal Crossing Nintendo 3DS bundle, tucked away in the back of the Toys R Us game section.

I’ve never played an Animal Crossing game and have to admit that the lack of farming bewildered me. What? How can I really enjoy personalizing and caring for my world if I’m not diligently watering radishes every day? Even when I started, I doubted I would really enjoy playing such a weird-looking human (the eyes too bright; the lower eyelashes so long!) rather than a cool, adorable animal. I started playing and suddenly it was two and a half hours later, exactly. (I know because of the 3DS Activity Log.)

So, what is compelling? For me, it’s the exploration: being new to the franchise, every swing of the net or fishing pole in the first couple weeks brought me something new and cool to look at, and I imagine this capitalistic wandering is something like what my childhood would have been like if I had been a boy, or allowed outside to play and examine the foliage around my house. Though I’m sure I’ll exhaust the novelty at some point, there’s also, like Harvest Moon, a sense of ichigo ichie that brings a preciousness even to the cicadas that are fucking everywhere, just because they’ll only be here until about mid-September or so — and then I’ll have brand new things to check out.

New Leaf also does a fantastic job of connecting you to other people, in part through the capabilities of the 3DS, which I’ve never appreciated until now. Through the camera, you can share and import designs that other people have made, pixel-art style, including outfits from Attack on Titan and Zelda games and paths you can lay down to mimic having more water in your town.

Additionally, entire towns or houses can be shared through the “Dreamsuite” or through Streetpass; the former I’ve used to visit some uncomfortably terrifying towns (such as the infamous Aika and Hitokui), and the latter I’m excited to use each night when I get back home, perusing the homes of people I’ve passed throughout the day and hoping for good furniture to hoard for rooms I don’t have yet. (Thus far I have both a “backyard” room and a “kaitenzushi and pastry restaurant” room planned…)

hito in…hitokui @__@
(“kui” means “to eat”)

Part of this novelty is the 3DS’s Streetpass itself — my digital headnod at the three people I Streetpass every morning on the bus — and part of it is just the weird delight of meeting people backward and inside-out — knowing, for example, that a fellow commuter is a strict interior designer and Nintendo fan (as shown by their vivid prize room), or that another also loves Attack on Titan (as shown by their “Scouting Legion” painting), before I know either of their names or genders.

I’m not too much of a social gamer — I don’t like playing competitively, and prefer to play games when I am completely alone — but I appreciate this variety of socialness, or rather not-aloneness, a way to get to interact with and get to know people in a low-stress, low-troll atmosphere.

I’ve started reserving my Tumblr for more “slice of life” updates and it’s there that you can find more screenshots of my Shiba adventures, as well as my encounters with my villagers, who are acquiring quite the vocabulary.

Warm Bodies, Isaac Marion

Jun 3rd, 2013

(source)
R is a young man with an existential crisis—he is a zombie. He has no memories, no identity, and no pulse, but he has dreams. His ability to connect with the outside world is limited to a few grunted syllables, but his inner life is deep, full of wonder and longing.

After experiencing a teenage boy’s memories while consuming his brain, R makes an unexpected choice that begins a tense, awkward, and strangely sweet relationship with the victim’s human girlfriend. Julie is a blast of color in the otherwise dreary and gray landscape that surrounds R. His choice to protect her will transform not only R, but his fellow Dead, and perhaps their whole lifeless world.

Scary, funny, and surprisingly poignant, Warm Bodies explores what happens when the cold heart of a zombie is tempted by the warmth of human love.

I read Warm Bodies a couple weekends ago and it was positively delightful. Every other page I was waving my arms in A’s direction and begging him to listen to me read aloud. “Zombie romance” does seem to be the next logical step after “vampire romance” and “werewolf romance,” but I never thought it would be this fun.

[spoilers ahead]

The only thing that fell flat for me was the character of Julie, who wavered a little too far on the side of the Dreaded Manic Pixie Dream Girl for me, and her quirks — keeping a wall in her room for “the future,” adopting a nickname of “Julie Cabernet” instead of “Julie Grigio” — were not just “meh,” but also didn’t contribute noticeably to the plot. The fact that she came to be considered as So Important to the healing of zombies by the book’s end also wavered uncomfortably on Mary Sue-ness.

Other than that, Warm Bodies was tons of fun. Marion’s detail on the “culture” of zombies — the way years pass in a haze, the way some zombies become skeletal “Boneys,” the way hunger resonates through their cells and how they are sated only with living brains and flashes of memory — were fantastic. So too were the little details of his world’s apocalypse: the way some people miss the sound of airplanes, for example, or the way that stadiums have been disemboweled and remade into gigantic slums. Marion’s writing is also super poignant in illustrating the typical un-life of a zombie.

There is a chasm between me and the world outside of me. A gap so wide my feelings can’t cross it. By the time my screams reach the other side, they have dwindled into groans.

I was surprised to learn there are even more Warm Bodies books, and though I felt this first volume ended on a pretty satisfying note, I’m sure I’ll get to the others eventually.

Salt Sugar Fat, M. Moss, & Pandora’s Lunchbox, M. Warner

Jun 3rd, 2013

covers of Pandora’s Lunchbox and Salt Sugar Fat

I’ve recently finished Salt Sugar Fat: How the Food Giants Hooked Us by Michael Moss and Pandora’s Lunchbox: How Processed Food Took Over the American Meal by Melanie Warner, both of which gave a fascinating view on the history, development, and livelihood of processed foods. Suffice to say that processed foods are achieved through an incredible amount of user testing and design, the results of which understands so deeply the heart and chemical of human desire that game designs would probably blush if they could.

For example, did you know that Doritos can be made without the powder coating your fingers, and taste exactly the same — but that the manufacturers keep Doritos with the powder because they view it as part of the “experience” of eating Doritos? I am skeptical, but impressed by the idea.

Both books touch on how processed food conquered kitchens under the weakened defenses of working, busy parents. Salt Sugar Fat was an in-depth view on these ingredients and their uses through the industry; Pandora’s Lunchbox touched on these as well, but also explored trends like the use of soybeans in Everything, and research on the benefits of whole foods versus processed foods.

For example, did you know that a Japanese study fed two groups of rats food that was nutritionally identical, but one group got “normal” hard pellets and one received “puffed” pellets (like puffed cereal)? The rats that received “puffed” pellets ended up gaining a significant amount of weight over the other rats! Potentially because food calories are partially used in actually breaking down that food, and foods that are processed take up less calories to break down, in addition to allowing sugars and starches to enter the bloodstream more quickly than the body may be able to handle.

Rather than being wholly disgusted by processed foods, I find myself weirdly more interested and fascinated by them and the fact that their taste and texture has been so incredibly optimized for my body.

I’m also way more enthusiastic for farmer’s markets than I was before. To prove it, here’s a photo of my new arpakasso with some potatoes I obtained last Saturday!

i named him “babbu”

I recommend both books to people who have an interest in food as well as an interest in what can make humans enjoy things perhaps more than they should.

Omen Sits on a Fissure’s Edge, Where the Core of the Earth Once Stretched Up to Reach the Stars, and, Finding the Skyscape Out of Reach, Collapsed Its Crags on Veins of Silver and Opal.

Jun 2nd, 2013

While it gathered its strength, refugees and wrens came between the seams of obsidian to erect homes strung like beads between wires and bridges: a necklace of a town, a veil on the sky-staring bride.

Because of the earth’s longing, they say, things happen here not by science but by intention, and the citizens are fluent in the tongue of symbols. They set their calendars not to the sun or moon, but to the shadows of wrens whose flocks the wind sings into staffs and blots. They sequence their itineraries to the shapes of leaf-stripped branches. Those seeking counsel spin at the edge of the canyons and upon stopping take the advice of a mushroom spotted in the bramble — or a drop of rain falling on the right ring finger — or a whiff of windfallen apples fermenting on the jags. A child playing on the bridges might, upon seeing her ball wince away from a sharp stone, decide then that it’s time for her to travel the world and grow into herself.

But it is hard to leave the guidance of the wishing earth, to go to the villages where only words mean anything, and the denizens refuse to listen unless you hollow yourself like a shell and echo their noises back to them. Many soon return, stumbling back, their knees and knuckles embracing veins of silver and opal. They tip their heads back, and the clouds touch their cheeks with rain, and purr with thunder. When they return, they stay, and would only leave again to meet the stars.
Figment Theme 5/22/2013: There are so many cities in the world, and a nearly infinite number of fictional towns in addition. Pick an intangible noun (think “hope,” “friendship,” “fear,” “loathing,”) and write a short scene set in a town with that name.

The Night Circus, Erin Morgenstern

May 21st, 2013

(source)
The circus arrives without warning. No announcements precede it. It is simply there, when yesterday it was not. Within the black-and-white striped canvas tents is an utterly unique experience full of breathtaking amazements. It is called Le Cirque des Rêves, and it is only open at night.

But behind the scenes, a fierce competition is underway—a duel between two young magicians, Celia and Marco, who have been trained since childhood expressly for this purpose by their mercurial instructors. Unbeknownst to them, this is a game in which only one can be left standing, and the circus is but the stage for a remarkable battle of imagination and will. Despite themselves, however, Celia and Marco tumble headfirst into love—a deep, magical love that makes the lights flicker and the room grow warm whenever they so much as brush hands.

True love or not, the game must play out, and the fates of everyone involved, from the cast of extraordinary circus per­formers to the patrons, hang in the balance, suspended as precariously as the daring acrobats overhead.

Written in rich, seductive prose, this spell-casting novel is a feast for the senses and the heart.

So The Night Circus was fantastic. Morgenstern’s writing set such an atmosphere that wherever I read it I felt the black-and-white-striped tents rising all around me, the glow of closely-hanging lights.

I’ve taken recently to thinking about books as made of three pillars — world-building, plot, and actual execution. The Night Circus was beautifully written, and set in a beautiful world. I felt the plot itself was a little lacking — the two main characters fall into love perhaps too easily — the Epic Battletimez seemingly promised by the cover description never really happen.

But honestly, for me this was just fine. For a while The Night Circus cracked my head open and took me somewhere else, and it did a wonderful job of doing it.

The design of the book was also gorgeous. Monochrome sunbursts! Little glowing flecks! It fit the mood of the book so well, and was sooo pretty.

Prodigy, Marie Lu

May 21st, 2013

(source)
June and Day arrive in Vegas just as the unthinkable happens: the Elector Primo dies, and his son Anden takes his place. With the Republic edging closer to chaos, the two join a group of Patriot rebels eager to help Day rescue his brother and offer passage to the Colonies. They have only one request—June and Day must assassinate the new Elector.

It’s their chance to change the nation, to give voice to a people silenced for too long.

But as June realizes this Elector is nothing like his father, she’s haunted by the choice ahead. What if Anden is a new beginning? What if revolution must be more than loss and vengeance, anger and blood—what if the Patriots are wrong?

In this highly-anticipated sequel, Lu delivers a breathtaking thriller with high stakes and cinematic action.

[spoilers ahead]

I finished Prodigy this past weekend, and enjoyed it. June and Day had more distinctive voices, and some of my confusions I had with the previous book were addressed more clearly here. In particular, the history of the Republic and the Colonies and how they came to be in conflict with each other is finally revealed, and probably for the best in this book, rather than in Legend; I can’t imagine the needless bulk that would have been in the first book had all that exposition been thrown in there.

Prodigy’s subversions of cliche were fun; just when you think June has no idea she’s being tricked, she reveals that she knows even more than you do about what’s going on, with a cleverness that isn’t eye-rolling. I thought that June and Day’s role in the rebellion as Beloved of the People was a little boring — Mockingjay was enough exploration of the “war celebrity” for me — though June and Day’s story, despite everything, is a little brighter than Katniss’ battle angst.

Overall, the motivations for each character were pretty well-done in Prodigy. I wish there had been a little more pattern to it — for example, at some point Tess asks Day, “If it were between me and June, who would you save?” Day answers, and though Day does later make a choice between June and the rebellion, this question made me expect and want a high-stress situation where Day’s true feelings between Tess and June were pressured out of him.

Prodigy! More cheesecake to enjoy. I like the direction it took with making neither side of the Republic/Colonies war ideal — in fact, both sides were pretty repugnant — and I’m excited to see where the last book will go.

Palimpsest, Catherynne M. Valente

May 21st, 2013

(source)
In the Cities of Coin and Spice and In the Night Garden introduced readers to the unique and intoxicating imagination of Catherynne M. Valente. Now she weaves a lyrically erotic spell of a place where the grotesque and the beautiful reside and the passport to our most secret fantasies begins with a stranger’s kiss.…

Between life and death, dreaming and waking, at the train stop beyond the end of the world is the city of Palimpsest. To get there is a miracle, a mystery, a gift, and a curse—a voyage permitted only to those who’ve always believed there’s another world than the one that meets the eye. Those fated to make the passage are marked forever by a map of that wondrous city tattooed on their flesh after a single orgasmic night. To this kingdom of ghost trains, lion-priests, living kanji, and cream-filled canals come four travelers: Oleg, a New York locksmith; the beekeeper November; Ludovico, a binder of rare books; and a young Japanese woman named Sei. They’ve each lost something important—a wife, a lover, a sister, a direction in life—and what they will find in Palimpsest is more than they could ever imagine.

Being terribly hungry for more of Valente’s writing after reading Deathless, I chose Palimpsest to read next due to its focus on a city, a subject I have a weakness for ever since reading China Miéville.

All I really have to say about this book is wow. My feedback for it is similar to anything I had to babble about Deathless — excellent, poetic, gorgeous. Like Deathless, though the plot was a little flimsy, I didn’t mind that much; whereas Deathless was suspended in a fairytale haze that made everything easy to digest, Palimpsest had all the (appropriate) tones of a dreamland, in which things don’t make utter sense but are all imbued with truth and urgency.

Something else I appreciated about this book in particular was the character Amaya Sei. Though I’m not Japanese myself, I felt that the treatment of Sei as a character was great; she didn’t sink into stereotypes, she stood up on her own, and overall she was allowed to be a person rather than a caricature. It was also refreshing to read a story with such a variety of characters, diverse unabashedly and without slowing down to explain things: this person is mixed, this person is gay, and so on.

Palimpsest was fantastic.

Deathless, Catherynne M. Valente

May 21st, 2013

(source)
Koschei the Deathless is to Russian folklore what devils or wicked witches are to European culture: a menacing, evil figure; the villain of countless stories which have been passed on through story and text for generations. But Koschei has never before been seen through the eyes of Catherynne Valente, whose modernized and transformed take on the legend brings the action to modern times, spanning many of the great developments of Russian history in the twentieth century.

Deathless, however, is no dry, historical tome: it lights up like fire as the young Marya Morevna transforms from a clever child of the revolution, to Koschei’s beautiful bride, to his eventual undoing. Along the way there are Stalinist house elves, magical quests, secrecy and bureaucracy, and games of lust and power. All told, Deathless is a collision of magical history and actual history, of revolution and mythology, of love and death, which will bring Russian myth back to life in a stunning new incarnation.

I can’t even believe that I’ve never read anything by Catherynne Valente before. Her writing is so gorgeous that I found myself struggling to slow down, to digest every word. True to the reviews on the inside cover, Deathless reads like modern fairytale, and for that reason even the stranger things in the book — Marya’s scars like tiger stripes, her hair thick as snakes, her “twelve mothers” you never quite meet, the palaces she walks of twitching skin — sink and seep in easily, where in a less poetic book they might have instead made me wince. Similarly, things that might have bothered me in other books, I was fine with here. Years transpire believably in paragraphs. We leave Marya and return months later once she has already made friends we don’t know, which she soon loses and spends the rest of the book crying for. Totally cool with it.

I loved Valente’s use of fairytale-esque repetition, the minimalist exposition and description that was just barely there. It was just the right amount of description to push you off the edge of things, and let the rest of you fly away into a sort of landscape that your own mind painted into her sketches.

It occurs to me that this makes it sound as if you have to put a lot of effort into understanding things, but this isn’t quite the case. Reading Valente’s writing is like reading Tarot cards. You see the images — fill your mind with them — and all the intricacies come floating up on their own.

Does that make sense either? To be honest, there’s no description I have that can do justice; all I can say is that after reading this book I’ve become a seriose fan, and have already started for everything else she’s written.
